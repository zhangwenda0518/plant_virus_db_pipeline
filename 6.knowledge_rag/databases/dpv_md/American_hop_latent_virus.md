### Introduction

First recorded by [Probasco & Skotland (1976)](#ref4) and described by [Adams & Barbara (1982*a*)](#ref2). A virus with filamentous RNA-containing particles, c. 15 x 680 nm, frequently occurring in hop *(Humulus lupulus)* in North America. Transmitted by mechanical inoculation of sap and in the non-persistent manner by aphids.

### Main Diseases

Natural infection has been reported only from hop *(Humulus lupulus)* but
is not associated with any disease. The virus induced no symptoms in experimentally
inoculated British cultivars ([Adams & Barbara, 1982*a*](#ref2)) but caused a
faint ring-and-line pattern in an American seedling clone ([Probasco &
Skotland, 1976](#ref4)).

### Geographical Distribution

Common in USA (E. G. Probasco & C. B. Skotland, personal communication)
otherwise reported only in plant breeders’ introductions from USA to England
([Adams, 1980](#ref1)) and West Germany (A. Eppler, personal communication).

### Host Range and Symptomatology

Experimentally the virus infected 17 of 41 species in 7 of 13 families
([Adams & Barbara, 1982*a*](#ref2)). Symptoms were indistinct or absent in most
hosts.

*Diagnostic species: Chenopodium quinoa.*Chlorotic spots appear within *c*. 10 days in inoculated leaves ([Fig. 1](#fig01)) followed by a faint systemic veinbanding and chlorotic mottle in some of the first leaves to be invaded systemically. Systemic symptoms intensify with age ([Fig. 2](#fig02)). : *Datura stramonium.* Small necrotic lesions which often develop into necrotic ringspots ([Fig. 3](#fig03)) appear within *c*. 14 days in the inoculated leaves; no systemic infection. *Propagation species: Chenopodium quinoa*is suitable for maintaining cultures and as a source of virus for purification. *Assay species: Datura stramonium*can be used for local lesion assay.

### Strains

None reported.

### Transmission by Vectors

In a single experiment the aphid *Phorodon humuli* transmitted the
virus from hop to
hop in the non-persistent manner ([Adams & Barbara, 1982*a*](#ref2)).

### Transmission through Seed

No reports.

### Serology

Antisera with tube precipitin titres of up to 1/800 were obtained by
injecting rabbits intramuscularly and/or subcutaneously with preparations of
intact particles. Extracts from infected hop and *Chenopodium quinoa* 
react at dilutions well in excess of 10-3 in direct ELISA ([Adams
& Barbara, 1982*a*](#ref2)) and no cross-reactions occur with the other two
[carlaviruses](/dpv/showdpv/?dpvno=259) common in hop, [hop mosaic](/dpv/showdpv/?dpvno=241) and [hop latent](/dpv/showdpv/?dpvno=261) .
Antibody coating of virus particles is useful for
quick identification by electron microscopy particularly in mixed infections
([Fig. 5](#fig05)).

### Relationships

The properties of American hop latent virus place it in the [carlavirus group](/dpv/showdpv/?dpvno=259).
No serological relationship to any of 15 carlaviruses was found in tube precipitin
tests or by direct ELISA, but F(ab')2-based indirect ELISA revealed a
distant relationship to nerine latent virus and very distant relationships to
[chrysanthemum B](/dpv/showdpv/?dpvno=110), [hop latent](/dpv/showdpv/?dpvno=261) and [hop mosaic](/dpv/showdpv/?dpvno=241) viruses; no relationship was found
to [carnation latent](/dpv/showdpv/?dpvno=061), [helenium S](/dpv/showdpv/?dpvno=265), [lily symptomless](/dpv/showdpv/?dpvno=096), [narcissus latent](/dpv/showdpv/?dpvno=170), [poplar
mosaic](/dpv/showdpv/?dpvno=075), [potato M](/dpv/showdpv/?dpvno=087), [potato S](/dpv/showdpv/?dpvno=060), [shallot latent](/dpv/showdpv/?dpvno=250) or [red clover vein mosaic](/dpv/showdpv/?dpvno=022) viruses
([Adams & Barbara, 1982*a*](#ref2), [1982*b*](#ref3)).

### Stability in Sap

No information.

### Purification

Up to 30 mg virus/kg of inoculated *C. quinoa* leaves can be obtained
by the following procedure. Use 0.05 M Tris-HCl buffer, pH 7.5, throughout.
Harvest inoculated leaves after *c.* 3 weeks and extract in buffer
(4 ml/g leaf) containing 0.016 M sodium sulphite. Clarify the extract by
addition of 1/5 vol of chloroform, then precipitate the virus by adding
polyethylene glycol (M. Wt 6000) to 6% and sodium chloride to 0.6%. The virus
in the resuspended pellets may be further purified by differential
centrifugation and sucrose density gradient centrifugation ([Adams &
Barbara, 1982*a*](#ref2)).

### Properties of Particles

Purified preparations show one component in sucrose density gradient
centrifugation.

*A*260/*A*280: 1.21 (corrected for
light-scattering; [Adams & Barbara 1982*a*](#ref2)).

### Particle Structure

Particles are straight or slightly flexuous filaments *c.* 15 x 680 nm.

### Particle Composition

*Nucleic acid:* RNA, single-stranded, *c.* 5-7% of the particle
weight (estimated from the *A*260/*A*280 ratio). M. Wt *c.*3.0 x 106, estimated by polyacrylamide gel electrophoresis under
non-denaturing conditions ([Adams & Barbara, 1982*a*](#ref2)).

*Protein: c.* 93-95% of the particle weight (estimated from the
*A*260/*A*280 ratio). A single polypeptide species, M. Wt
*c.* 32,700, estimated by SDS/polyacrylamide gel electrophoresis ([Adams
& Barbara, 1982*a*](#ref2)).

### Relations with Cells and Tissues

No information.

### Notes

American hop latent virus is distinct from the two other [carlaviruses](/dpv/showdpv/?dpvno=259)
known to occur in hop, [hop mosaic](/dpv/showdpv/?dpvno=241) and [hop latent](/dpv/showdpv/?dpvno=261);
it is only very distantly related to them serologically,
has a wider host range, and differs from them in causing obvious symptoms in
several species, notably *Datura stramonium* and *Chenopodium quinoa,*both of which are immune to hop latent virus and sustain only symptomless
local infection by hop mosaic virus. Symptoms caused by the latter two viruses
in herbaceous hosts are too mild for diagnosis, and identification of all three
viruses, which can occur together in hop, is best done by serology.

Outside North America, American hop latent virus has been found only in
recent introductions to W. Germany and England, indicating that it may be a
relatively new pathogen of North American hop plants.
