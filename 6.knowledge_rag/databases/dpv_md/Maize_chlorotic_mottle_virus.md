### Introduction

Disease and virus were originally described by [Castillo & Hebert (1974)](#ref4) and [Hebert & Castillo (1973)](#ref8). A virus with small isometric RNA-containing particles *c.* 30 nm in diameter which sediment as a single component. It causes chlorotic mottling, necrosis and stunting of maize, with poor or no ear development and premature plant death. The vectors are chrysomelid beetles which retain ability to transmit the virus for up to 6 days. The virus is transmissible by mechanical inoculation, and infects only species of Gramineae. Reported from Peru, Argentina, Mexico and USA but economically important only in limited coastal areas of Peru and in Kansas and Nebraska, USA.

### Main Diseases

The virus causes a variety of symptoms in maize (*Zea mays*), depending upon genotype,
age of infection and environmental conditions. They range from a relatively mild
chlorotic mottle
to severe stunting, leaf necrosis, premature plant death, shortened male inflorescences
with few
spikes, and/or shortened, malformed, partially filled ears
([Castillo Loayza, 1977](#ref2);
[Castillo & Hebert, 1974](#ref4);
[Niblett & Claflin, 1978](#ref16);
[Uyemoto *et al.,* 1981](#ref27)).
The virus caused crop
losses of 10-15% in natural field infections and losses of up to 59% in experimental
maize plots
([Castillo Loayza, 1977](#ref2)).

In the USA, the virus acts synergistically with
[maize dwarf mosaic virus](/dpv/showdpv/?dpvno=341) strains A or B, or
with
[wheat streak mosaic virus](/dpv/showdpv/?dpvno=048),
to produce the economically damaging corn lethal necrosis
disease
([Niblett & Claflin, 1978](#ref16);
[Uyemoto, Bockelman & Claflin, 1980;](#ref26)
[Uyemoto *et al.,* 1981](#ref27));
crop losses as high as 91% have been reported
([Niblett & Claflin, 1978](#ref16)).
Leaves of corn plants inoculated early (three to seven leaf stage) with both maize chlorotic
mottle virus and maize dwarf mosaic virus turn chlorotic then necrotic and plants become stunted,
die prematurely, and bear small, deformed and partially filled or no ears
([Uyemoto *et al.,* 1981](#ref27)).
Plants inoculated later (14-leaf stage) are not stunted but terminal leaves develop
chlorosis and
necrosis
([Uyemoto *et al.,* 1981](#ref27)) and ears are of
normal size but kernel weight is greatly
reduced. The virus complex was also associated with some late-infected plants that showed
normal green foliage but bore prematurely yellowed and necrotic ears with shrivelled kernels
([Uyemoto *et al.,* 1980](#ref26)).

### Geographical Distribution

The virus has been reported only in the Western Hemisphere countries of Peru
([Castillo Loayza, 1977](#ref2);
[Castillo & Hebert, 1974;](#ref4)
[Hebert & Castillo, 1973](#ref8);
[Nault *et al.,* 1979](#ref14)),
Argentina
([Teyssandier, Nome & Dalbo, 1983](#ref21)),
Mexico (D. T. Gordon & O. E. Bradfute,
unpublished data) and the USA
([Doupnik, Lane & Wysong, 1982](#ref5);
[Niblett & Claflin, 1978](#ref16);
[Uyemoto *et al.,* 1980](#ref26)). It is probably present in other Western Hemisphere
countries wherever its chrysomelid vectors are found in maize growing areas. Its verified
field vectors (*Diabrotica* species) occur only in the New World
([Krysan & Branson, 1983](#ref10)).

### Host Range and Symptomatology

The experimental host range is restricted to the Gramineae (Poaceae) with maize as a natural host.
In mechanical inoculations, both Kansas and Peru serotypes systemically infected *Bromus*spp., *Digitaria sanguinalis, Eragrostis trichodes, Hordeum* spp.,
*Panicum* spp., *Setaria* spp., *Sorghum* spp., *Triticum aestivum* 
([Bockelman, Claflin & Uyemoto, 1982](#ref1);
[Castillo & Hebert, 1974](#ref4);
[Niblett & Claflin, 1978](#ref16)) and *Zea mays* ssp. *mays* 
and *mexicana* 
([Castillo & Hebert, 1974](#ref4);
[Nault *et al.,* 1982](#ref15)).
The Kansas serotype
1 also infected *Z. mays* ssp. *parviglumis* and *Z. luxurians* 
but not *Z. perennis* 
and *Z. diploperennis* 
([Nault *et al.,* 1982](#ref15)). Dicotyledonous species were not infected when
mechanically inoculated
([Castillo & Hebert, 1974](#ref4);
[Niblett & Claflin, 1978](#ref16)).

*Diagnostic species: Zea mays*(maize). Numerous dent inbreds and hybrids as well as sweet corn and popcorn types are very susceptible. Symptoms include chlorotic mottling ([Fig. 1](#fig01)), leaf necrosis and plant stunting ([Castillo Loayza, 1977](#ref2); [Uyemoto *et al.,* 1981](#ref27)). Tolerant genotypes develop chlorotic mottle or mosaic, or latent infections. These genotypes may also be susceptible to the mechanically transmissible [maize dwarf mosaic](/dpv/showdpv/?dpvno=341) and [wheat streak mosaic](/dpv/showdpv/?dpvno=048) viruses, which cause symptoms indistinguishable from those induced by maize chlorotic mottle virus. : *Sorghum bicolor* (sorghum) cv. Asgrow Bugoff. Immune. Also immune to wheat streak mosaic virus. This sorghum cultivar is useful in distinguishing the virus from maize dwarf mosaic virus strain A, which causes chlorotic mottle or red leaves, and strain B, which causes intense systemic leaf reddening ([Uyemoto *et al.,* 1980](#ref26)). : *Triticum aestivum* (wheat) cv. Parker. Occasionally develops chlorotic spots and rarely a faint systemic mottle. This host is useful in differentiating the virus from wheat streak mosaic virus, which causes systemic chlorotic streaks, and from maize dwarf mosaic virus, which causes no infection ([Uyemoto *et al.,* 1980](#ref26)). *Propagation and assay species*: Maize inbreds H84, N28, N28Ht and Oh28, maize hybrid PM-205, and the sweet corn cultivar Aristogold Bantam Evergreen served as virus assay or propagation hosts for studies on mechanical and beetle transmission and on virus characterisation ([Bockelman *et al.,* 1982](#ref1); [Castillo & Hebert, 1974](#ref4); [Ferguson, Shahin & Uyemoto, 1978](#ref6); [Nault *et al.,* 1978](#ref13); [Niblett & Claflin, 1978](#ref16); [Phillips, Uyemoto & Wilson, 1982](#ref20); [Uyemoto *et al.,* 1980](#ref26), [1981](#ref27)) or for virus purification (R. E. Gingery, unpublished data; [Niblett & Claflin, 1978](#ref16); [Uyemoto, 1980](#ref22)). No reliable local lesion assay host has been reported.

### Strains

Three strains or serotypes have been reported: Kansas serotypes 1 and
2
([Uyemoto *et al.,* 1980](#ref26))
and the Peru serotype
([Niblett & Claflin, 1978](#ref16);
[Niblett, Claflin & Hebert, 1977](#ref18)).
All three serotypes were readily differentiated by formation of
spurs in double immunodiffusion tests in agar gels
([Niblett & Claflin, 1978](#ref16);
[Niblett *et al.,* 1977](#ref18);
[Uyemoto, 1980](#ref22));
in addition, the Kansas serotype 1 and the Peru serotype were
differentiated by enzyme-linked immunosorbent assay (ELISA)
([Uyemoto, 1980](#ref22))
and differential
host responses
([Bockelman *et al.,* 1982](#ref1)).
Both Kansas serotypes were detected in
Nebraska (J. K. Uyemoto, unpublished data). The serotypes of the isolates from Argentina and
Mexico have not been determined.

### Transmission by Vectors

Adults of six species of chrysomelid beetle transmitted
the virus experimentally in the USA
([Jensen, 1979](#ref9);
[Nault & Knoke, 1981](#ref12);
[Nault *et al.,* 1978](#ref13)):
*Diabrotica virgifera virgifera* (western corn rootworm)
([Fig. 2](#fig02)),
*D. barberi*
(*=longicornis*) (northern corn rootworm), *D. undecimpunctata howardi* 
(southern corn
rootworm), *Chaetocnema pulicaria* (corn flea beetle),
*Systena frontalis* (a flea beetle)
and *Oulema melanopa* (cereal leaf beetle). In Peru, adults of *D. viridula* and *D. decempunctata sicuanica* were vectors
([Castillo, 1983](#ref3)).
Larval forms of *D. undecimpunctata* 
([Jensen, 1979](#ref9)),
*D. virgifera virgifera, D. barberi* (S. G. Jensen, personal communication),
*D. viridula* 
([Castillo, 1983](#ref3))
and *O. melanopa* 
([Nault *et al.,* 1978](#ref13))
also transmitted
the virus. Only *Diabrotica* species have been implicated as vectors in the field.
They occur
only in the New World, most species having a tropical distribution
([Krysan & Branson, 1983](#ref10)),
whereas the experimental vectors *O. melanopa* and *C. pulicaria* occur worldwide.
Both adult and larval forms of *Diabrotica* spp. appear important in virus epidemiology
([Uyemoto, 1983*a*](#ref23),
[1983*b*](#ref24)).

The virus was acquired and inoculated by beetles after feeding periods of less than 24 h
each
([Nault & Knoke, 1981](#ref12)).
It persisted in adult *D. virgifera virgifera* for 3 days
and in *O. melanopa* for 4-6 days at room temperature
([Nault *et al.,* 1978](#ref13)).
There
is no latent period in beetles
([Jensen, 1979](#ref9)).
Adult *O. melanopa,* developed from
viruliferous larvae, failed to transmit
([Nault *et al.,* 1978](#ref13)),
suggesting that beetle-transmissible
virus neither is retained in the gut and regurgitated during feeding nor does
it multiply or circulate
in the vector to be transmitted via the saliva.

### Transmission through Seed

The virus was not transmitted through seed of maize
inbreds (2153 seedlings) or hybrids (1898 seedlings)
([Bockelman *et al.,* 1982](#ref1))
or hybrid
PM-205 (800 seedlings)
([Castillo Loayza, 1977](#ref2);
[Castillo & Hebert, 1974](#ref4))
nor through seed of
*Panicum miliaceum* (842 seedlings), *Setaria lutescens* (135 seedlings) or
*S. viridis* 
(2676 seedlings)
([Bockelman *et al.,* 1982](#ref1)).

### Serology

The virus is moderately to highly immunogenic. Antisera were produced in rabbits by
intramuscular injections, at 7-day intervals, of purified virus emulsified in Freund's complete
or incomplete adjuvant
([Castillo & Hebert, 1974](#ref4);
[Niblett & Claflin, 1978](#ref16);
[Paul *et al.,* 1980](#ref19))
or by a combination of intramuscular and intravenous injections spaced at 10-day
intervals
([Uyemoto, 1980](#ref22)).
The antisera had titres of 1/512 to 1/2048 in gel double diffusion
tests
([Paul, Querfurth & Huth, 1980](#ref19);
[Uyemoto, 1980](#ref22)),
homologous reactants producing
a single precipitin line
([Uyemoto & Claflin, 1981](#ref25)).
Serological tests most commonly used
are gel double diffusion
([Bockelman *et al.,* 1982](#ref1);
[Castillo & Hebert, 1974](#ref4);
[Nault *et al.,* 1978](#ref13);
[Niblett & Claflin, 1978](#ref16);
[Paul *et al.,* 1980](#ref19);
[Phillips *et al.,* 1982](#ref20):
[Uyemoto, 1980](#ref22);
[Uyemoto *et al..* 1980](#ref26),
[1981](#ref27)) and ELISA
([Bockelman *et al.,* 1982](#ref1);
[Nault *et al.,* 1982](#ref15);
[Phillips *et al.,* 1982](#ref20);
[Teyssandier *et al.,* 1983](#ref21);
[Uyemoto, 1980](#ref22);
[Uyemoto *et al.,* 1980](#ref26)).
Other tests employed are microprecipitin
assay
([Nault *et al.,* 1978](#ref13),
[1979](#ref14),
[1982](#ref15)),
immunoelectrophoresis
([Paul *et al.,* 1980](#ref19)),
immunosorbent electron microscopy
([Lesemann & Paul, 1980](#ref11))
and
immunofluorescence
([Gingery *&* Gordon, 1981](#ref7);
[Nault *et al.,* 1979](#ref14)).

### Relationships

Maize chlorotic mottle virus resembles a number of other viruses that have host ranges confined
to the Gramineae, high infectivity titres, high thermal inactivation points and
isometric particles of
*c.* 28 nm diameter, and that are easily transmitted mechanically, sediment as single
components, and contain *c.* 21% RNA
([Paul *et al.,* 1980](#ref19)).
However, it is unrelated
serologically to several such viruses:
[brome stem leaf mottle](/dpv/showdpv/?dpvno=107),
[cocksfoot mild mosaic](/dpv/showdpv/?dpvno=107),
[cocksfoot mottle](/dpv/showdpv/?dpvno=023),
cynosurus mottle,
[holcus transitory mottle](/dpv/showdpv/?dpvno=107),
[molinia streak](/dpv/showdpv/?dpvno=177),
[panicum mosaic](/dpv/showdpv/?dpvno=177),
[phleum mottle](/dpv/showdpv/?dpvno=107)
and
[St Augustine decline](/dpv/showdpv/?dpvno=177) viruses
([Castillo & Hebert, 1974](#ref4);
[Hebert & Castillo, 1973](#ref8);
[Niblett & Claflin, 1978](#ref16);
[Paul *et al.,* 1980](#ref19)).
It is also unrelated serologically to
[brome mosaic](/dpv/showdpv/?dpvno=180),
[rice yellow mottle](/dpv/showdpv/?dpvno=149)
and
[sowbane mosaic](/dpv/showdpv/?dpvno=064) viruses
([Castillo & Hebert, 1974](#ref4);
[Hebert & Castillo, 1973](#ref8);
[Niblett & Claflin, 1978](#ref16)),
[barley stripe mosaic](/dpv/showdpv/?dpvno=344),
[foxtail mosaic](/dpv/showdpv/?dpvno=264),
[maize dwarf mosaic](/dpv/showdpv/?dpvno=341) and
[wheat streak mosaic](/dpv/showdpv/?dpvno=048) viruses
([Niblett & Claflin, 1978](#ref16)),
and to
[maize chlorotic dwarf](/dpv/showdpv/?dpvno=194),
[maize rayado fino](/dpv/showdpv/?dpvno=220),
[maize streak](/dpv/showdpv/?dpvno=133),
[maize stripe](/dpv/showdpv/?dpvno=300)
and
[maize white line mosaic](/dpv/showdpv/?dpvno=283) viruses
(D. T. Gordon, unpublished data). It has most properties in common with the
sobemoviruses
or with the
viruses related to cocksfoot mild mosaic virus.

### Stability in Sap

Sap from infected maize had a thermal inactivation point of 80-85°C
([Castillo Loayza, 1977](#ref2);
[Castillo & Hebert, 1974](#ref4);
[Ferguson *et al.,* 1978](#ref6);
[Hebert & Castillo, 1973](#ref8);
[Uyemoto & Claflin, 1981](#ref25)), a dilution end-point of 10-6
([Castillo Loayza, 1977](#ref2);
[Castillo & Hebert, 1974](#ref4);
[Hebert & Castillo, 1973](#ref8))
or 10-8
([Ferguson *et al.,* 1978](#ref6);
[Uyemoto & Claflin, 1981](#ref25))
and a longevity *in vitro* at 20°C
greater than 12 days
([Castillo Loayza, 1977](#ref2);
[Castillo & Hebert, 1974](#ref4))
or 33 days
([Ferguson *et al.,* 1978](#ref6);
[Uyemoto & Claflin, 1981](#ref25)).

### Purification

The following methods gave good yields (up to 1 mg/g fresh weight tissue).

1. Adaptations of the
[Niblett & Paulsen (1975)](#ref17)
method for
[panicum mosaic virus](/dpv/showdpv/?dpvno=177)
([Ferguson *et al.,* 1978](#ref6);
[Niblett & Claflin, 1978](#ref16);
[Uyemoto, 1980](#ref22)).
Blend infected
maize leaves in cold 0.1 M potassium phosphate, pH 7.0, plus 1% 2-mercaptoethanol
(1 g tissue/3 ml buffer) and strain through cheesecloth. Clarify extract by slowly adding an
equal volume of cold chloroform/butanol (1:1, v/v) with gentle stirring. Incubate emulsion
for 30 min at 4°C and recover aqueous phase by centrifugation (5000 ***g***,
7 min). Filter through glass wool and pellet virus particles (147,000 *g,* 2 h); suspend the
pellets in 0.02 M phosphate buffer, pH 7, and further purify by two cycles of differential
centrifugation (12,000 ***g***, 10 min; 269,000 ***g***, 90 min);
resuspend finally in 0.02 M phosphate (2.5 ml/ 100 g). For large extract
volumes, centrifuge (12,000 ***g***, 10 min) and precipitate the virus by adding
polyethylene glycol (PEG), M. Wt 6000, to 8% (w/v) and NaCl to 0.2 M (2 h, room temperature)
and recover precipitate (8000 ***g***, 10 min). Suspend in 0.1 M phosphate buffer,
clarify with cold chloroform:butanol (1:1, w/v), and concentrate the virus by two cycles of
differential centrifugation. Additional purification is achieved by rate-zonal centrifugation
([Castillo & Hebert, 1974](#ref4);
[Niblett & Claflin, 1978](#ref16))
or zone electrophoresis
([Ferguson *et al.,* 1978](#ref6);
[Uyemoto, 1980](#ref22)).

2. (R. E. Gingery, unpublished data). Blend infected maize leaves in
0.2 M K2HPO4, adjusted to pH 5.0 with 0.1 M citric acid,
plus 0.5% 2-mercaptoethanol
(1 g tissue/ml
buffer). Clarify with 0.25 vol. chloroform and precipitate virus from the aqueous phase
by adding
PEG to 6% (w/v) and KCl to 4% (w/v). Recover precipitate by centrifugation
(10,000 ***g***,
20 min) and further purify by rate-zonal sucrose density-gradient centrifugation and
isopycnic
banding in CsCl.

### Properties of Particles

Purified virus preparations contain a single sedimenting component.

Sedimentation coefficient, *s*20,w: 109 S
([Niblett & Claflin, 1978](#ref16);
[Niblett *et al.,* 1977](#ref18)),
not adjusted for infinite dilution.

*A*260/*A*280: 1.69-1.73 (R. E. Gingery, unpublished data).

Buoyant density in CsCl: 1.365 g/cm3 (R. E. Gingery, unpublished data).

### Particle Structure

Particles were isometric, *c.* 30 nm in diameter when negatively stained in
potassium phosphotungstate, pH 6.9
([Fig. 3](#fig03))
(O. E. Bradfute, unpublished data;
[Castillo Loayza, 1977](#ref2);
[Castillo & Hebert, 1974](#ref4))
and *c.* 33 nm when stained in uranyl acetate
([Fig. 4](#fig04))
(O. E. Bradfute, unpublished data).
Many particles were hexagonal in outline and some were penetrated by stain.
The detailed particle structure is unknown.

### Particle Composition

*Nucleic acid:* RNA, probably single-stranded, with M. Wt 1.4-1.6 x 106
([Ferguson *et al.,* 1978](#ref6);
R. E. Gingery, unpublished data;
[Niblett *et al.,* 1977](#ref18)).
Constitutes *c.* 18% of the particle weight
(R. E. Gingery, unpublished data).
Molar percentage of nucleotides: G22-25; A24-26; C15-16; U34-37
(R. E. Gingery, unpublished data),

*Protein:* Single migrating protein species, of M. Wt 23.9-27.0 x 103
([Ferguson *et al.,* 1978](#ref6);
[Niblett *et al.,* 1977](#ref18);
[Paul *et al.,* 1980](#ref19)),
constituting *c.* 82% of the particle weight.

*Other components:* None reported.

### Relations with Cells and Tissues

No studies reported.

### Notes

Two other mechanically transmissible viruses that commonly infect maize are
[maize dwarf mosaic virus](/dpv/showdpv/?dpvno=341)
and
[wheat streak mosaic virus](/dpv/showdpv/?dpvno=048).
Maize chlorotic mottle virus may be distinguished from these two filamentous viruses
by the reactions of sorghum and wheat and by particle morphology.
Maize chlorotic mottle virus is easily
differentiated from other viruses commonly infecting maize in the Western Hemisphere
([maize chlorotic dwarf virus](/dpv/showdpv/?dpvno=194),
[maize mosaic virus](/dpv/showdpv/?dpvno=094),
[maize rayado fino virus](/dpv/showdpv/?dpvno=220),
[maize stripe virus](/dpv/showdpv/?dpvno=300) and
[maize white line mosaic virus](/dpv/showdpv/?dpvno=283)),
because none of these is mechanically transmissible. All except the last,
which is soil-borne, are transmitted
by either leafhoppers or planthoppers. Among viruses rarely identified from diseased maize
in the Western
Hemisphere,
[brome mosaic virus](/dpv/showdpv/?dpvno=180)
and
[cucumber mosaic virus](/dpv/showdpv/?dpvno=213)
are mechanically transmissible but have quite
different host ranges from that of maize chlorotic mottle virus, and
[barley yellow dwarf virus](/dpv/showdpv/?dpvno=032)
is obligately aphid transmitted.
