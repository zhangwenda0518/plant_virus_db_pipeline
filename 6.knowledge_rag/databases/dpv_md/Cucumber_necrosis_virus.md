### Introduction

Described by [McKeen (1959)](#ref5) and [Dias & Doanne (1968)](#ref4). An RNA-containing virus with isometric particles *c.* 31 nm in diameter. Soil-borne, transmitted by the chytrid fungus *Olpidium cucurbitacearum* and readily transmitted by mechanical inoculation to a wide range of plants. Found naturally only in greenhouse cucumbers.

### Main Diseases

Causes necrotic spots, severe malformation of the leaves and
stunting in greenhouse cucumbers.

### Geographical Distribution

Canada.

### Host Range and Symptomatology

Several species of Amaranthaceae, Chenopodiaceae,
Compositae, Cucurbitaceae, Leguminosae and Solanaceae can be infected by mechanical
inoculation of sap. Usually only inoculated leaves become infected and buff or
necrotic local lesions appear 2-5 days after inoculation. The virus becomes systemic
only in *Cucumis sativus* (cucumber) and, less frequently, in *Vigna sinensis*(cowpea) and *Zinnia elegans;* it does not become fully systemic in other
Cucurbitaceae.

*Diagnostic species: Cucumis sativus*(cucumber). Necrotic lesions ([Fig. 3](#fig03)) develop in inoculated cotyledons in 3-4 days and enlarge to 3-5 mm; the cotyledons desiccate and die. Systemic symptoms may include: chlorotic or tan-coloured areas with pin-point necrotic centres which usually fall out leaving ‘shot-holes’ of various sizes ([Fig. 4](#fig04)); severely deformed leaves, sometimes with dark green enations; and small fruits, occasionally with a green mottle, developing on the stunted plants. Symptoms in summer are mild or indistinct. : *Gomphrena globosa.* Irregular greyish necrotic local lesions with reddish margins develop in 3-5 days ([Fig. 2](#fig02)). : *Chenopodium amaranticolor.* Local necrotic lesions (0.5-1 mm) develop in 2-5 days ([Fig. 1](#fig01)), the margins reddening as the leaves age. Heavily infected leaves desiccate and fall. : *Nicotiana tabacum* (tobacco). Greyish necrotic local spots up to 4 mm in diameter ([Fig. 5](#fig05)). *Propagation species: Cucumis sativus*cv. Windermoor Wonder is a good host for maintaining cultures. Highly infectious inoculum is obtained from cotyledons 5-7 days after inoculation. *Assay species: Chenopodium amaranticolor*and *Gomphrena globosa* are good local lesion hosts.

### Strains

No strain differences reported.

### Transmission by Vectors

Transmitted in soil by zoospores of the chytrid
fungus *Olpidium cucurbitacearum* 
([Barr, 1968](#ref1);
[Dias, 1970*a*](#ref2)).
The virus
is not carried internally by the resting spores of the fungus; virus-free zoospores
are discharged both from resting spores and zoosporangia
([Fig. 6](#fig06))
formed in
virus-infected roots. Virus released from roots adsorbs to zoospores after a 5-15 min
contact period and enters roots at the same time as the zoospores
([Dias, 1970*b*](#ref3)).
The fungus appears to infect only species of the Cucurbitaceae. *Olpidium
brassicae,* the vector of
[tobacco necrosis virus](/dpv/showdpv/?dpvno=014),
does not transmit cucumber
necrosis virus nor does *O. cucurbitacearum* transmit tobacco necrosis virus
([Dias, 1970*b*](#ref3)).
Attempts to transmit the virus by the melon aphid (*Aphis
gossypii*) failed
([McKeen, 1959](#ref5)).

### Transmission through Seed

Not seed-transmitted in cucumber
([McKeen, 1959](#ref5)).

### Transmission by Dodder

Not reported.

### Serology

Antisera with titres greater than 1/1000 are readily obtained by
intramuscular injection. The virus forms a single precipitin band near the antigen
well in gel-diffusion tests. This test reliably detects virus in crude sap.

### Relationships

Cucumber necrosis virus is not serologically related to
[tobacco necrosis virus](/dpv/showdpv/?dpvno=014)
([Dias & Doanne, 1968](#ref4))
although both viruses have many similar
properties. Cucumber necrosis virus should not be confused with viruses of the same
name from the Netherlands
([Van Koot & Van Dorst, 1955](#ref9)),
Sweden
([Rydén, 1966](#ref6)) and possibly USSR
([Vovk & Nikiforova, 1961](#ref10)),
which are strains of tobacco
necrosis virus.

### Stability in Sap

In tobacco sap, the thermal inactivation point (10 min) is
75-80°C, the dilution end-point is 10-4-10-5 and the virus
retains infectivity for about 30 days at room temperature. Very little infectivity
is lost after 1 year in frozen sap or in desiccated leaves
([McKeen, 1959](#ref5)).
The virus
is precipitated by 40% saturated ammonium sulphate and the infectivity of clarified
extracts is unaffected between pH 4 and 8
([Dias & Doanne, 1968](#ref4)).

### Purification

The following method gives highly infectious preparations:
blend 100 g of fresh or frozen leaves with 200 ml 0.67 M sodium phosphate buffer pH
7.0 and 50 ml 0.1 M ascorbic acid, filter the sap through gauze and centrifuge at
low speed (12,500 ***g***). Clarify by adding 1/10th vol. chloroform followed
by acidification (pH 5.3), and differential centrifugation
([Dias & Doanne, 1968](#ref4)),
or by adding ammonium sulphate to 40% saturation and subjecting the precipitate to
differential centrifugation. Additional purification can be obtained by rate zonal
density gradient centrifugation.

### Properties of Particles

[Dias & Doanne (1968)](#ref4)
reported one component in
purified preparations analysed by rate-zonal centrifugation in sucrose density
gradients.
[Tremaine (1970)](#ref7)
detected an accessory component in crude preparations
which sedimented at one-third the rate of the main component.

Sedimentation coefficient (*s*20, w): 133 S
([Dias & Doanne, 1968](#ref4)).

Molecular weight: 8.6 x 106 (J. H. Tremaine, unpublished).

Diffusion cofficient: 1.20 x 10-7 cm2/sec (J. H. Tremaine,
unpublished).

Isoelectric point: pH 3.9 (J. H. Tremaine, unpublished).

### Particle Structure

Particles are isometric, *c.* 31 nm in diameter
([Fig. 7](#fig07)),
apparently angular in outline.

### Particle Composition

16% RNA and 84% protein (J. H. Tremaine, unpublished).
Approximately 391 amino acid residues per subunit. Amino acid composition: ala 41;
arg 17; asx 46; cys 0; glx 24; gly 32; his 3; ile 20; leu 33; lys 16; met 1; phe 20;
pro 23; ser 32: thr 31: trp 7; tyr 12; val 33
([Tremaine & Goldsack, 1968](#ref8)).

### Relations with Cells and Tissues

No information.

### Notes

The properties and host range of cucumber necrosis virus and
[tobacco necrosis virus](/dpv/showdpv/?dpvno=014),
especially the cucumber strains, are similar; therefore, serological
tests are the only reliable way of identifying the viruses.
