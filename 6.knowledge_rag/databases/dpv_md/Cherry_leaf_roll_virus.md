### Introduction

The virus was described as elm mosaic by [Swingle *et al*. (1941](#ref55),[1943](#ref56)) and
[Varney & Moore (1952)](#ref59) and as cherry leaf roll by [Posnette & Cropley
(1955)](#ref41) and [Cropley (1961)](#ref13). Symptoms of walnut blackline disease, which is now
also known to be caused by this virus ([Mircetich & Rowhani, 1984](#ref36)), were first
described by [Schuster & Miller (1933)](#ref54) but not then attributed to an infective
agent.

*Synonyms*: None. Many strain names used.

A virus with isometric particles *c*. 28 nm in diameter, common in many wild
and cultivated woody plant species in Europe, the USSR, North America and New
Zealand. It is readily transmitted by mechanical inoculation of sap and by seed
to progeny of naturally and experimentally infected plants. It has a wide
experimental host range and shows many features of nepoviruses but appears not
to be transmitted by nematodes. There is evidence that at least some strains may
be transmitted in pollen to the plant pollinated.

### Main Diseases

Causes leaf patterns and blackline disease of walnut ([Savino
*et al*., 1977](#ref45); [Cooper, 1979](#ref6), [1980](#ref7); [Mircetich *et al*., 1980](#ref37); [Mircetich &
Rowhani, 1984](#ref36); [Fig. 1](#fig01)); chlorotic mosaic, ring patterns and dieback in American
elm ([Swingle *et al*., 1943](#ref56); [Mayhew & Epstein, 1971](#ref35)); leaf rolling and plant
death in cherry ([Cropley, 1961](#ref13); [Schimanski *et al*., 1975](#ref47); [Fig. 2](#fig02)); chlorotic
ringspot, leaf patterns and/or yellow vein netting in *Betula* spp. ([Schmelzer,
1972*a*](#ref52); [Cooper & Atkinson, 1975](#ref8); [Fig. 3](#fig03)), *Sambucus* spp. ([Schmelzer,
1966](#ref51);
[Schimanski & Schmelzer, 1972](#ref46); [Hansen & Stace-Smith, 1971](#ref20)), *Rubus* spp.
([Cropley & Tomlinson, 1971](#ref14); [Jones, 1976](#ref23); [Jones & Wood, 1978](#ref27)) and
*Cornus florida* ([Waterworth & Lawson, 1973](#ref65)); and yellow spotting of *Ptelea
trifoliata* ([Schmelzer, 1972*b*](#ref53)). It is associated with ringspot symptoms in
privet
([Schmelzer, 1972*b*](#ref53)) and, when present with [arabis mosaic virus](/dpv/showdpv/?dpvno=016), with chlorotic
ringspot of lilac ([Novak & Lanzova, 1975](#ref40)). Symptomless natural infection
occurs in olive ([Savino & Gallitelli, 1981](#ref44)), rhubarb ([Tomlinson &
Walkey, 1967](#ref57)), *Berteroa incana* ([Lockhart, 1977](#ref32)), *Delphinium elatum*
([Ahmed &
Bailiss, 1975](#ref1)) and *Rumex obtusifolius* ([Walkey & Cooper, 1973](#ref60)).

### Geographical Distribution

Found in Europe and North America ([Cropley &
Tomlinson, 1971](#ref14)), Turkey ([Kurcman, 1977](#ref30)),
the USSR ([Bubaker & Pomazkov, 1978](#ref2))
and New Zealand ([Jones & Wood, 1978](#ref27)).

### Host Range and Symptomatology

All isolates tested have wide experimental
host ranges; hosts are reported in more than 36 plant families ([Schmelzer, 1966](#ref51);
[Hansen & Stace-Smith, 1971](#ref20); [Horvath, 1979](#ref21)), including the monocotyledons
*Tinantia erecta* and *Commelina* spp. ([Schmelzer, 1966](#ref51); [Horvath, 1979](#ref21)).

*Diagnostic species*: *Chenopodium amaranticolor, C. quinoa.* Chlorotic or necrotic local lesions followed by systemic mottle or necrosis and distortion. : *Cucumis sativus* (cucumber). Chlorotic local lesions in cotyledons followed occasionally by systemic mosaic. : *Nicotiana rustica, N. tabacum* (tobacco) cvs White Burley, Xanthi-*nc*. Necrotic local lesions in 3-4 days, frequently developing into concentric necrotic rings ([Fig. 5](#fig05)); systemic necrotic or chlorotic rings and line-patterns depending on the virus strain and growing conditions. The plants usually recover to produce symptomless new leaves. *Propagation species*: *Nicotiana rustica* or *N. tabacum* cvs White Burley and Xanthi-*nc* are suitable for maintaining virus cultures. *N. clevelandii* and *Chenopodium quinoa* are the species most often used for propagating the virus for purification. *Assay species*: *Nicotiana tabacum* cv. White Burley or Xanthi-*nc* and *Chenopodium amaranticolor* are good local lesion hosts.

### Strains

Many strains have been reported. Isolates from different natural
host species are serologically distinguishable from each other but most isolates
from a single host species are not ([Fig. 4](#fig04); [Jones & Murant, 1971](#ref26); [Jones,
1976](#ref23)). However, isolates of more than one serological type have been reported from
privet ([Schmelzer, 1972*b*](#ref53)), dogwood ([Walkey *et al*., 1973](#ref64)), walnut
([Savino *et al*.,
1977](#ref45); [Cooper & Edwards, 1980](#ref9)) and American elm
(B. Lockhart, personal communication). Isolates that cannot be
differentiated serologically may differ in virulence both in natural and
experimental hosts. The following are the strains that have been most studied:

: Type (cherry) strain ([Cropley, 1961](#ref13)).: Elm mosaic strain ([Varney & Moore, 1952](#ref59)).: Rhubarb strain ([Tomlinson & Walkey, 1967](#ref57)).: Golden elderberry strain ([Hansen & Stace-Smith, 1971](#ref20)).: Red elder ringspot strain ([Schmelzer, 1972*b*](#ref53)).: Dogwood ringspot strain ([Waterworth & Lawson, 1973](#ref65)).: Birch strain ([Cooper & Atkinson, 1975](#ref8)).: Walnut ringspot and walnut yellow vein strains ([Savino *et al*., 1977](#ref45)).: Blackberry and red raspberry strains ([Cropley & Tomlinson, 1971](#ref14); [Jones & Wood, 1978](#ref27)).

[Jones (1976)](#ref23) found that six strains could be grouped serologically on the
basis of geographical distribution. The dogwood ringspot and elm mosaic strains
from the USA formed one group and European strains from cherry, birch,
blackberry and possibly rhubarb, formed the other. Analysis of the RNA species
of some of these strains by cDNA hybridization has supported this grouping
([Massalski, 1984](#ref33)).

### Transmission by Vectors

Transmission of cherry isolates by the nematodes
*Xiphinema coxi, X. diversicaudatum* and *X. vuittenezi* was reported
([Fritzche &
Kegler, 1964](#ref17); [Flegg, 1969](#ref15)). This has not been substantiated in more rigorous
tests with the cherry, golden elderberry and rhubarb strains, nor were these
strains transmitted by *X. americanum, X. bakeri, Longidorus elongatus, L.
leptocephalus, L. macrosoma* or *Paralongidorus maximus* ([van Hoof, 1971](#ref58);
[Jones *et al*., 1981](#ref29)). The elm mosaic strain was not
transmitted by *X. americanum* ([Fulton & Fulton, 1970](#ref18)) or by the aphid
*Myzus persicae* ([Ford *et al*., 1972](#ref16)).

### Transmission through Seed

Seed-borne (0.5-35%) in most natural
hosts tested, including American elm ([Callahan, 1957*a*](#ref3), [1957*b*](#ref4)), *Betula* spp. ([Cooper,
1976](#ref5); [Schimanski *et al*., 1980](#ref49)), *Prunus* spp. ([Schimanski *et al*.,
1975](#ref47), [1976](#ref48)), rhubarb ([Tomlinson & Walkey, 1967](#ref57)), *Sambucus* spp. ([Schimanski &
Schmelzer, 1972](#ref46)) and walnut ([Quacquarelli & Savino, 1977](#ref42); [Cooper &
Edwards, 1980](#ref9)). The elm mosaic and birch strains are transmitted both through
pollen and through the ovule to the seed of their natural hosts ([Callahan,
1957*a*](#ref3), [1957*b*](#ref4); [Cooper *et al*., 1984](#ref11)). There is some evidence in
birch that infected pollen introduced the virus into the pollinated tree ([Cooper
*et al*., 1984](#ref11)). Strong circumstantial evidence indicates that spread of the
walnut strain in mature Californian walnut orchards is through pollen ([Mircetich
*et al*., 1980](#ref37)); up to 14% of healthy walnut trees hand-pollinated with infected
pollen became infected (S. M. Mircetich & A. Rowhani, unpublished data).
Seed-borne up to 100% in many herbaceous hosts ([Schmelzer, 1965](#ref50); [Lister &
Murant, 1967](#ref31); [Hansen & Stace-Smith, 1971](#ref20); [Cooper, 1976](#ref5); [Murant, 1983](#ref38)).

### Transmission by Dodder

An elderberry strain was not transmitted by *Cuscuta
californica, C. campestris* or *C. subinclusa* ([Schmelzer, 1966](#ref51)).

### Serology

The virus is moderately immunogenic giving rabbit antiserum
with titres up to 1/2000. In gel double diffusion tests the virus produces one
band of precipitate and in spring it can be detected by this means in cherry sap
([Cropley, 1960](#ref12)). Enzyme-linked immunosorbent assay and immunosorbent electron
microscopy have been used to detect infection in tissues of natural and
experimental hosts ([Jones & Duncan, 1980](#ref24); [Cooper *et al*.,
1984](#ref11); [Massalski & Cooper, 1984](#ref34); [Rowhani *et al*., 1985](#ref43)).

### Relationships

The properties of its particles place cherry leaf roll virus in the [nepovirus](/dpv/showdpv/?dpvno=185)
group but it appears not to be transmitted by nematodes ([Jones *et al*., 1981](#ref29)). [Varney
& Moore (1952)](#ref59) found that the elm mosaic strain failed to induce lesions in plants
previously infected with [tomato ringspot virus](/dpv/showdpv/?dpvno=290). [Jones (1976)](#ref23) confirmed this
observation with eight strains of cherry leaf roll virus in *Nicotiana tabacum*
cv. Xanthi-*nc* but found that none of the strains protected plants against
lesion production by tomato ringspot virus. Although the results of their plant
protection tests led [Varney & Moore (1952)](#ref59)
to suspect a relationship with tomato
ringspot virus, serological tests have shown the viruses to be distinct ([Fulton &
Fulton, 1970](#ref18); [Jones & Murant, 1971](#ref26); [Jones & Duncan, 1980](#ref24)). Furthermore, no
serological relationship was detected to the nepoviruses [grapevine Bulgarian latent](/dpv/showdpv/?dpvno=186),
[lucerne Australian latent](/dpv/showdpv/?dpvno=225) or [peach rosette mosaic](/dpv/showdpv/?dpvno=150) viruses, which all have RNA-2
species of M. Wt similar to that of cherry leaf roll virus ([Jones & Duncan, 1980](#ref24)),
nor to [arabis mosaic](/dpv/showdpv/?dpvno=016), [grapevine fan leaf](/dpv/showdpv/?dpvno=028), [raspberry ringspot](/dpv/showdpv/?dpvno=198), [strawberry latent
ringspot](/dpv/showdpv/?dpvno=126), [tobacco ringspot](/dpv/showdpv/?dpvno=309),
[tomato black ring](/dpv/showdpv/?dpvno=038), tomato ringspot or tomato tip
necrosis viruses ([Cropley, 1961](#ref13); [Hansen & Stace-Smith, 1971](#ref20); [Jones & Murant, 1971](#ref26)).

### Stability in Sap

In sap of infected *Nicotiana clevelandii* or *N. tabacum*, infectivity
is lost after 10 min at 50-60°C, dilution to 10-3-10-5 or
storage at 20°C for 4-16 days ([Cropley, 1961](#ref13); [Walkey *et al*., 1973](#ref64); [Jones,
1976](#ref23)).
Strains from elm and dogwood seem less stable than most other strains ([Jones, 1976](#ref23)).
In elm pollen, the elm mosaic
strain was infective after 3 years at 0°C ([Callahan, 1957*b*](#ref4)).

### Purification

Some strains are more difficult to purify than others. The following method
was successful in purifying several strains ([Jones, 1973](#ref22); [Cooper & Atkinson, 1975](#ref8)).

Extract infected *Chenopodium quinoa* or *Nicotiana clevelandii*
leaves in 0.5 M borate buffer, pH 6.5 (1 g leaf: 2 ml buffer), freeze the slurry
overnight, thaw and centrifuge at 10,000 ***g*** for 10 min. Add 15 g
granulated ammonium sulphate to each 100 ml supernatant fluid and stir overnight
at 4°C. Centrifuge at 10,000 ***g*** for 10 min and concentrate the virus
from the supernatant fluid by differential centrifugation and sucrose density
gradient centrifugation.

An alternative method ([Walkey *et al*., 1973](#ref64)) is to extract leaves in 0.5
M sodium citrate buffer, pH 6.5, and
chloroform (1 g:1 ml:1 ml). Add polyethylene glycol (M. Wt 6000) to the aqueous
phase to 6% (w/v) and stir for 2 h
before centrifuging at 10,000 ***g*** for 10 min. Resuspend the
virus-containing pellet in 0.01 M Tris-HCl, pH 7.3.
Concentrate the virus by differential centrifugation and sucrose density gradient centrifugation.

### Properties of Particles

Particles in purified preparations sediment as two nucleoprotein components (M, B).
Preparations of some strains also contain an RNA-free component (T). The ratio of M:B
component differs with the virus strain ([Jones, 1973](#ref22); [Haber & Hamilton, 1980](#ref19)).

Sedimentation co-efficients (*s*20,*w*, svedbergs): 52 (T),
115 (M), 128 (B).

Particle weights (daltons x 10-6): 3.24 (T), 5.53 (M), 6.06 (B).

Diffusion coefficient (D20,*w*): calculated to be 1.48 x
10-7 cm2/s ([Murant *et al*., 1981](#ref39)).

Electrophoretic mobility: at pH 6.5 the elm mosaic and dogwood ringspot strains
migrated to the anode whereas the cherry, golden elderberry and rhubarb strains
migrated to the cathode. All five strains appeared to contain two electrophoretic
components; these did not correspond to the M and B sedimenting components ([Walkey
*et al*., 1973](#ref64)).

*A*260/*A*280: 1.62 (mixture of M and B
components).

Buoyant density in CsCl (g/cm3): 1.47 (M), 1.50 (B).

### Particle Structure

Isometric, *c*. 28 nm in diameter, angular in outline ([Fig. 7](#fig07)). A proportion
of the particles is penetrated by negative stain ([Fig. 7](#fig07)), and these presumably
correspond to the T component.

### Particle Composition

*Nucleic acid*: RNA, single-stranded; two genomic species, RNA-1 and RNA-2,
packaged separately in B particles and M particles respectively, and comprising 46%
(B) and 41% (M) of the particle weight. In polyacrylamide gels under non-denaturing
conditions their estimated M. Wt was 2.4 x 106 (RNA-1) and 2.1 x
106 (RNA-2) ([Jones & Mayo, 1972](#ref25); [Walkey *et al*., 1973](#ref64)). In agarose
gels the estimated M. Wts of glyoxal-denatured RNA molecules were 2.82 x 106
and 2.29 x 106 respectively ([Murant *et al*., 1981](#ref39)). In the golden
elderberry strain, the molar percentage of nucleotides for RNA-1 was G26.5; A22.0;
C22.3; U29.1. For RNA-2 it was G26.6; A21.4; C22.8; U29.2 ([Walkey *et al*.,
1973](#ref64)).

*Protein*: In polyacrylamide/SDS gels, protein preparations from all
strains studied contain a single polypeptide species of estimated M. Wt 54,000
([Jones & Mayo, 1972](#ref25); [Walkey *et al*.,1973](#ref64)). The particles are assumed to
contain 60 such subunits which comprise 59% and 54% of the weights of M and B
particles respectively ([Jones & Mayo,
1972](#ref25); [Murant *et al*., 1981](#ref39)). The amino-acid compositions of the particle
proteins of five strains have been determined ([Walkey *et al*., 1973](#ref64)).

### Genome Properties

Both RNA species are required for infectivity ([Jones &
Mayo, 1972](#ref25); [Jones & Duncan, 1980](#ref24); [Haber & Hamilton, 1980](#ref19)). They both
contain polyadenylate sequences ([Massalski, 1984](#ref33)) and a genome-linked protein of
estimated M. Wt 3500 (C. U. T. Hellen & J. I. Cooper, *personal
communication*). In pseudo-recombinant isolates made between the golden
elderberry and rhubarb strains, RNA-2 determined serological specificity, the
ratio of M : B components and the production of T component; RNA-1 determined
the ability to infect *Gomphrena globosa* ([Jones & Duncan, 1980](#ref24); [Haber &
Hamilton, 1980](#ref19)). [Haber & Hamilton (1980)](#ref19) found that RNA-2 determined the
type and severity of symptoms in species of *Chenopodium* and *Nicotiana*
whereas
[Jones & Duncan (1980)](#ref24) found that RNA-1 was the major determinant of these
characters but that RNA-2 had a modifying influence. Determinants on both RNA-1
and RNA-2 were involved in plant protection ([Jones & Duncan, 1980](#ref24)).

### Relations with Cells and Tissues

In *Nicotiana* spp. the virus was detected
within tubules in pollen, ovules, mature seeds, root and shoot meristems and
leaf cells ([Fig. 6](#fig06); [Walkey & Webb, 1968](#ref61), [1970](#ref62); [Jones *et al*.,
1973](#ref28)). Leaf cells also contained inclusion bodies consisting of membraneous
structures, endoplasmic reticulum and ribosomes ([Jones *et al*.,
1973](#ref28)). In naturally infected plants, virus-like particles within tubules were
detected in leaf cells ([Cooper & Atkinson, 1975](#ref8)), in the unfertilized female
gametophyte and embryo of birch ([Cooper *et al*., 1984](#ref11)), and in
pollen grains of birch ([Massalski & Cooper, 1984](#ref34)). Virus was also detected
by ELISA on the surface of pollen grains from infected birch, cherry and walnut
([Massalski & Cooper, 1984](#ref34)). Virus-like particles were never detected in the
nucleus of infected cells. Infected cells often possessed cell wall projections
([Jones *et al*., 1973](#ref22); [Cooper & Atkinson, 1975](#ref8)); in
symptom-bearing leaves of birch, chloroplasts often contained large densely
staining plastoglobuli and had poorly defined lamellae ([Cooper & Atkinson,
1975](#ref8)).

The virus can be eliminated from plants by meristem tip culture ([Walkey et
al., 1969](#ref63)) and by maintaining seedlings for 7 days at 40°C or 20 days at 32°C
([Cooper & Walkey, 1978](#ref10)).

### Notes

In natural and experimental host plants the virus induces symptoms
resembling those produced by many [nepoviruses](/dpv/showdpv/?dpvno=185) and can therefore be identified
unequivocally only by serological tests. Because of the wide range of
serological variants, antisera to several virus strains should be used for
identification.
