### Introduction

Described by [Lind (1915)](#ref15), [Robbins (1921)](#ref17) and [Smith (1957)](#ref18). *Selected synonyms: Beta*virus 2 (*Rev. appl. Mycol.* **17**: 52): *Marmor betae* (*Rev. appl. Mycol.* **19**: 229) A virus with flexuous filamentous particles *c.* 730 nm long and 13 nm wide. Transmitted by many species of aphids in the non-persistent manner and fairly easily by sap inoculation. Host range moderately wide. Widely distributed throughout the beet-growing areas of the world.

### Main Diseases

Causes a mosaic disease in *Beta vulgaris* (sugar beet, red beet,
spinach beet, etc.) and in *Spinacea oleracea* (spinach).

### Geographical Distribution

World-wide in major beet-growing areas, especially in temperate regions.

### Host Range and Symptomatology

Host range is moderately wide, mainly in the Chenopodiaceae, Solanaceae and
Leguminosae; species in about 10 dicotyledonous families have been infected
experimentally ([Bennett, 1949](#ref3)). Transmitted readily by aphids or by sap
inoculation to the following species:

*Diagnostic species: Beta vulgaris*(sugar beet). Young leaves often show vein-clearing ([Fig. 1](#fig01)). Older leaves show a pronounced light and dark green mottle and are often puckered ([Fig. 3](#fig03)). Infected plants are sometimes stunted but severe distortion of the leaves is uncommon. : *Spinacea oleracea* (spinach). Numerous, small yellow flecks, often coalescing to form large chlorotic areas, appear on the youngest leaves. Older leaves become progressively chlorotic and necrotic, and infected plants are usually stunted. *Propagation species*: Sugar beet is ideal for maintaining cultures. *Assay species*: Sugar beet and spinach are good hosts for testing aphids and for whole-plant assay using sap inoculation. *Beta patellaris* is a useful local lesion host, developing a few large necrotic lesions ([Fig. 2](#fig02)). *Amaranthus retroflexus*and *A. caudatus* also give local lesions ([Bennett, 1949](#ref3)). Many chlorotic local lesions are produced in *Chenopodium quinoa* ([Fig. 4](#fig04)) and in *Gomphrena globosa.*

### Strains

Many minor variants, differing in virulence towards sugar beet, have been
described ([Bennett, 1964](#ref4)). Beet water mottle virus ([Watson, 1958](#ref22)) is probably
a variant.

### Transmission by Vectors

Transmissible by more than 28 aphid species ([Kennedy, Day & Eastop, 1962](#ref14))
but *Myzus persicae* and *Aphis fabae* are the principal vectors in
the field. Transmission is of the non-persistent type ([Watson, 1946](#ref21); [Sylvester,
1949](#ref19)) and is improved by starving aphids for 2-5 min before acquisition feeds.
Acquisition and inoculation thresholds are 6-10 sec; no latent period.
Persistence of the virus in the vector depends on the species of aphid
([Sylvester, 1952](#ref20)) but is probably less than 1 hr in apterae. All instars
transmit but alatae transmit more efficiently than apterae and retain virus
for up to 4 hr ([Cockbain, Gibbs & Heathcote, 1963](#ref9)). The virus is not
transmitted to progeny of vectors and vectors do not retain virus after
moulting.

### Transmission through Seed

Probably not seed-transmitted.

### Transmission by Dodder

Three species of *Cuscuta* did not transmit ([Bennett, 1944](#ref2)).

### Serology

The virus is only moderately immunogenic ([Bercks, 1960](#ref5)); antisera with titres
greater than 1/1000 are difficult to obtain. Tube and droplet precipitin tests
with clarified sap or partially purified virus preparations have been used.

### Relationships

A member of the [potato virus Y group](/dpv/showdpv/?dpvno=245) of viruses ([Brandes & Bercks, 1965](#ref7))
and serologically distantly related to [bean yellow mosaic virus](/dpv/showdpv/?dpvno=040) and
[potato virus Y](/dpv/showdpv/?dpvno=242)
([Bercks, 1960](#ref5)).

### Stability in Sap

In beet sap, thermal inactivation point (10 min) is between 55 and 60°C,
dilution end-point is up to 1/4000 and infectivity is retained for 24-48 hr at
20°C ([Bennett, 1964](#ref4)). In beet leaves, infectivity survived 1 year at
-20°C ([Yamaguchi, 1964](#ref24)).

### Purification

The virus seems rather unstable *in vitro;* partially purified
preparations have been obtained using the following method ([Chod &
Polák, 1969](#ref8)). Extract frozen beet leaves in phosphate buffer (pH 7.8)
containing 0.01 M veronal, 0.01 M cysteine and 0.007 M ethylenediamine-tetraacetate. Centrifuge at low speed. Precipitate the virus from the supernatant
fluid by adding a 40% aqueous solution of polyethylene glycol (PEG) to reach a
final PEG concentration of about 10%, and allowing the mixture to stand for 30
min. Sediment and clarify by differential centrifugation, resuspending the
pellets obtained at high speed in 0.01 M phosphate buffer.

### Properties of Particles

Extraction of leaves of infected sugar beet or *Stellaria media* with
water-saturated phenol yielded preparations which infected *Chenopodium
amaranticolor* ([Beiss, 1963](#ref1)).

### Particle Structure

Flexuous, filamentous particles ([Fig. 5](#fig05)) about 730 nm long and 13 nm in
diameter ([Zimmer & Brandes, 1956](#ref25)).

### Relations with Cells and Tissues

In beet, vesicular X-bodies are produced in the cytoplasm, and crystalline
inclusions occur in the chloroplasts ([Fujisawa, Matsui & Yamaguchi, 1967](#ref11));
nucleoli are enlarged and distorted in beet ([Bos, 1969](#ref6)) and in *Gomphrena
globosa* ([Martelli & Russo, 1969](#ref16)); cytoplasm contains inclusions which
appear in section as ‘pinwheels’ and bundles and are digestible by pepsin but
not by ribonuclease ([Hoefert, 1969](#ref13)).

### Notes

Beet mosaic virus is usually of little economic importance in sugar beet or
spinach; its effects on yield of sugar beet are very small ([Wiesner, 1959](#ref23)). The
symptoms it causes in sugar beet might be confused with the yellow mosaic and
blotching caused by [tomato black ring](/dpv/showdpv/?dpvno=038) and [tobacco rattle](/dpv/showdpv/?dpvno=346) viruses ([Gibbs &
Harrison, 1964](#ref12)), though these two viruses are readily distinguished from beet
mosaic virus by their symptoms in other hosts, the morphology of their particles,
and by having nematode vectors. Among other viruses occurring in sugar beet and
spinach, [cucumber mosaic](/dpv/showdpv/?dpvno=213) and beet ring mottle viruses ([Duffus & Costa, 1963](#ref10))
cause mottling, pronounced chlorosis, narrowing and distortion of the leaves,
and severely stunt the plants; [beet yellows](/dpv/showdpv/?dpvno=013), [beet mild yellowing](/dpv/showdpv/?dpvno=089) and [beet western
yellows](/dpv/showdpv/?dpvno=089) viruses all cause chlorosis of the older leaves but not mottling or
chlorotic flecking; and [beet curly top virus](/dpv/showdpv/?dpvno=210) induces leaf-curling, wart-like
protuberances on the leaves, and stunting of the plant.
