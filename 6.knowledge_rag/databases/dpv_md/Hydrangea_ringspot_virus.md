### Introduction

Described by [Brierley (1954)](#ref4) and [Brierley & Lorentz (1957)](#ref5). A virus with filamentous particles of normal length *c.* 490 nm. Readily transmitted by inoculation of sap; no vector known. World-wide distribution.

### Main Diseases

Many naturally infected *Hydrangea macrophylla* plants carry
the virus without showing conspicuous symptoms. Crinkling, rolling and asymmetry of
leaves
([Fig. 1](#fig01)),
decrease in number of florets per inflorescence, severe dwarfing and
‘running out’ of hydrangea varieties have all been attributed
to the presence of the virus.
Experimentally infected hydrangeas may develop chlorotic or brown spots or rings on the
leaves and may show distortion and buckling of the leaves several months after inoculation
([Brierley & Lorentz, 1957](#ref5);
[Hollings, 1958](#ref7)).
Symptom expression probably depends on
variety and environmental conditions.

### Geographical Distribution

Reported from USA, Europe and New Zealand. Probably
world-wide in cultivated *Hydrangea macrophylla.*

### Host Range and Symptomatology

*Hydrangea macrophylla* seems to be the only
natural host. Experimentally, the virus causes infections in more than 20 species in 12
dicotyledonous families. It infects *Primula malacoides* systemically
([Hollings, 1958](#ref7)),
often without inducing symptoms.

*Diagnostic species: Gomphrena globosa.* Local necrotic dots within 3-5 days, later enlarging and developing red margins ([Fig. 4](#fig04)). : *Chenopodium quinoa.* Local chlorotic spots within 5-7 days ([Fig. 2](#fig02)) sometimes becoming bright yellow or necrotic or developing into chlorotic ringspots. : *Chenopodium amaranticolor.* Local chlorotic dots with a central necrotic speck within 5-7 days. *Propagation species: Primula malacoides.* Naturally infected hydrangeas can also be used as a source of virus for purification. *Assay species: Gomphrena globosa* is the most sensitive indicator plant. *Chenopodium quinoa* and *C. amaranticolor* may also be used.

### Strains

Minor variants can be distinguished on the basis of serology
([Hollings, 1958](#ref7);
[Belli & Belli, 1967](#ref1))
and symptom expression on *Chenopodium quinoa*
([Koenig, 1970](#ref8)).

### Transmission by Vectors

No vector known. Attempts to transmit the virus by the aphids *Myzus persicae* and
*Macrosiphum euphorbiae* failed
([Hollings, 1958](#ref7)).

### Transmission through Seed

Not found
([Brierley & Lorentz, 1957](#ref5)).

### Transmission by Dodder

Apparently not tested.

### Serology

The virus is strongly immunogenic, forming flagellar precipitates in tube or slide
precipitin tests.

### Relationships

Particle morphology and physical properties place the virus in the
[potexvirus group](/dpv/showdpv/?dpvno=200)
([Brandes & Bercks, 1965](#ref3)).
It is distantly serologically related to
[potato X](/dpv/showdpv/?dpvno=354),
[cactus X](/dpv/showdpv/?dpvno=058),
[white clover mosaic](/dpv/showdpv/?dpvno=041) and
[clover yellow mosaic](/dpv/showdpv/?dpvno=111) viruses.

### Stability in Sap

In sap from hydrangea, the thermal inactivation point (10 min) is between 70 and
75°C, the dilution end-point is about 10-5 and infectivity is retained
at room temperature for 2-3 weeks
([Brierley & Lorentz, 1957](#ref5);
[Hollings, 1958](#ref7)).

### Purification

1. Mince 100 g hydrangea leaves with 20 ml 0.5 M borate buffer pH 7.8, add 0.2 g sodium
sulphite and 0.2 g ascorbic acid and centrifuge expressed sap at low speed. Add 1 volume
0.1% silver nitrate to 6 volumes of the supernatant fluid, stir for 10 min and leave at room
temperature for several hours. Centrifuge at low speed, add 1 volume of 20% polyethylene
glycol 6000 in 0.5 M borate buffer, pH 7.8, to 4 volumes of the supernatant fluid, leave at
4°C for several hours. Centrifuge at low speed, resuspend the pellets in 15 ml of 0.5 M
borate buffer containing 0.5 M urea and 0.1% 2-mercaptoethanol. Subject the virus to 1 or
2 cycles of differential centrifugation (R. Koenig, unpublished).

2. The ether-carbon tetrachloride method of
[Wetter (1960)](#ref11)
can be used with extracts from
infected *Primula malacoides*
([Bercks & Brandes, 1961](#ref2)).

### Properties of Particles

Electrophoretic mobility: -3.9 x 10-5 cm2 sec-1
volt-1 in 0.05 M sodium phosphate buffer at pH 7.0
([Sisler *et al.,* 1957](#ref10)).

Absorbance at 260 nm (1 mg/ml, 1 cm light path): 3.08 (calculated from
[Sisler *et al.,* 1957](#ref10)).

*A*260/*A*280:
1.22 (calculated from
[Sisler *et al.,* 1957](#ref10)).

### Particle Structure

Particles are flexuous filaments
([Fig. 3](#fig03))
with normal length *c.* 490 nm
([Bercks & Brandes, 1961](#ref2)).

### Particle Composition

*Nucleic acid:* Probably single-stranded RNA for which a M. Wt of 2.5 x
106 is found by polyacrylamide gel electrophoresis (R. Koenig, unpublished).

*Protein:* SDS-polyacrylamide gel electrophoresis yields two bands corresponding
to M. Wt of 1.9 and 2.2 x 104. Thin layer gel chromatography with Sephadex
G 200 in the presence of 6 M guanidine hydrochloride and 0.1% 2-mercaptoethanol yields
a single spot corresponding to a M. Wt of about 2.4 x 104 (R. Koenig,
unpublished). As with the proteins of other
[potexviruses](/dpv/showdpv/?dpvno=200)
([Koenig, 1972](#ref9))
the meaning of
multiple band formation in SDS-polyacrylamide gel electrophoresis is still unclear.

### Relations with Cells and Tissues

No specific inclusions are seen in hydrangea, but chloroplasts are often clumped
together. Epidermal hair cells of infected *Primula malacoides* often contain oval
or elongated vacuolate bodies ranging from 13 x 32 to 11.5 x 50 µm. These inclusions
stain intensely with 0.5% phloxine and sometimes contain discrete granules
([Hollings, 1958](#ref7)).

### Notes

Hydrangea ringspot virus can be differentiated from other viruses infecting *Hydrangea
macrophylla (i.e.*
[tomato ringspot](/dpv/showdpv/?dpvno=290),
[tobacco ringspot](/dpv/showdpv/?dpvno=309),
[tobacco necrosis](/dpv/showdpv/?dpvno=014),
[cucumber mosaic](/dpv/showdpv/?dpvno=213)
and
[alfalfa mosaic](/dpv/showdpv/?dpvno=229) viruses)
by its inability to infect tobacco and other solanaceous hosts
(but see
[Dunez, 1963](#ref6))
and by its elongated shape.
