### Introduction

Described by [Brunt & Stace-Smith (1978*a*](#ref3), [1978*b*](#ref4)). *Possible synonym*: Cat’s ear yellow spot virus ([Schmidt, 1977](#ref10)) A virus with fragile rod-shaped particles *c.* 22 nm in diameter and predominant lengths of 120-140 and 220-240 nm. It is transmissible by mechanical inoculation of sap to species in eight families, but its vector is unknown. The virus occurs in eastern and western Canada, Australia and possibly also the Federal Republic of Germany.

### Main Diseases

*Hypochoeris radicata* (cat’s ear) and *Leontodon autumnalis* (autumnal or
fall hawkbit) are the only known natural hosts of the virus. Some leaves of infected *H.
radicata* plants are extensively chlorotic
([Fig. 1](#fig01)),
but others are either only slightly
chlorotic or symptomless
([Brunt & Stace-Smith, 1978*a*](#ref3)).
Young leaves of *L.
autumnalis* develop chlorotic spots below 15°C (minimum daily temperature), but
symptoms are less conspicuous at higher temperatures and are usually absent at 25°C
and above.

### Geographical Distribution

Prevalent in *Hypochoeris radicata* in scattered locations in western Canada
([Brunt & Stace-Smith, 1978*a*](#ref3)),
Australia
([Greber & Finlay, 1981](#ref5))
and possibly also in
the German Democratic Republic
([Zschau, 1964](#ref14)).
Locally common in *Leontodon autumnalis* 
in eastern Canada
([Singh & McDonald, 1980](#ref11)).

### Host Range and Symptomatology

In addition to *Hypochoeris radicata* and *Leontodon autumnalis,* the virus
infected 16 of 56 species from 7 of 12 families
([Brunt & Stace-Smith, 1978*a*](#ref3);
[Singh & McDonald, 1980](#ref11);
[Greber & Finlay, 1981](#ref5)).

*Diagnostic species: Chenopodium amaranticolor*and *C. quinoa.* Inoculated leaves develop chlorotic lesions ([Fig. 2](#fig02), [Fig. 3](#fig03)) which sometimes extend along the veins and later become necrotic. Systemically infected leaves develop a faint chlorosis. : *Chenopodium murale.* Necrotic lesions in inoculated leaves; no systemic infection. : *Cucumis sativus* (cucumber). The Australian isolate induces a few chlorotic lesions in inoculated leaves, and chlorotic mottling and distortion of systemically infected leaves. Not infected by the type isolate. Susceptibility to the isolate from eastern Canada not tested. : *Gomphrena globosa.* The isolate from eastern Canada induces chlorotic lesions and veinal necrosis in inoculated leaves, and the development of abnormally small systemically infected leaves with necrotic lesions. Not infected by the type isolate. Susceptibility to the Australian isolate not tested. : *Montia perfoliata.* The isolate from western Canada induces severe chlorosis of systemically infected leaves, but that from Australia causes symptomless systemic infection only. Susceptibility to the isolate from eastern Canada not tested. : *Nicotiana clevelandii.* Inoculated leaves develop a few grey necrotic lesions or oakleaf patterns ([Fig. 4](#fig04)), followed by veinal necrosis and death. Systemically infected leaves are much reduced in size but are only faintly chlorotic. Plants are severely stunted. : *Tetragonia expansa.* A few chlorotic lesions in inoculated leaves; no systemic infection. *Propagation species: Nicotiana clevelandii*is useful for maintaining cultures, and is the best source of virus for purification. *Assay species: Chenopodium amaranticolor*and *C. quinoa* can be used as local lesion assay hosts.

### Strains

None reported; isolates from different geographical areas have slightly different host
ranges and induce different symptoms in a few common hosts
([Brunt & Stace-Smith, 1978*a*](#ref3);
[Singh & McDonald, 1980](#ref11);
[Greber & Finlay, 1981](#ref5)).

### Transmission by Vectors

Vector unknown. Not transmitted in the non-persistent manner by any of four aphid
species (*Aphis gossypii, Aulacorthum solani, Macrosiphum euphorbiae* and *Myzus
persicae),* or by the stubby-root nematode (*Nanidorus minor)* 
([Brunt & Stace-Smith, 1978*a*](#ref3);
[Greber & Finlay, 1981](#ref5)).

### Transmission through Seed

Not seed-borne in *Hypochoeris radicata, Nicotiana clevelandii* or *Chenopodium
quinoa* 
([Brunt & Stace-Smith, 1978*a*](#ref3);
[Greber & Finlay, 1981](#ref5)).

### Serology

The virus is a moderately good immunogen. Antiserum obtained from a rabbit 30 days
after the second of two injections, each of *c.* 100 µg purified virus emulsified
with Freund’s complete adjuvant, had a titre of 1/1280 in tube precipitin and
microprecipitin
tests and 1/2560 in ring interface tests
([Brunt & Stace-Smith, 1978*a*](#ref3)).

### Relationships

The properties of the virus suggest that it is a possible member of the
[tobamovirus](/dpv/showdpv/?dpvno=184) group,
but it is serologically unrelated to
[tobacco mosaic virus](/dpv/showdpv/?dpvno=151)
and to the following six labile
tobamoviruses:
[broad bean necrosis](/dpv/showdpv/?dpvno=223),
[beet necrotic yellow vein](/dpv/showdpv/?dpvno=144),
[peanut clump](/dpv/showdpv/?dpvno=235),
[potato mop-top](/dpv/showdpv/?dpvno=138),
[nicotiana velutina mosaic](/dpv/showdpv/?dpvno=189)
and
[wheat soil-borne mosaic](/dpv/showdpv/?dpvno=077).
It is also unrelated to
[barley stripe mosaic](/dpv/showdpv/?dpvno=344) and
[tobacco rattle](/dpv/showdpv/?dpvno=346) viruses
([Brunt & Stace-Smith, 1978*a*](#ref3)).

### Stability in Sap

In *Nicotiana clevelandii* sap the virus loses infectivity after 10 min at 45-50°C,
1-2 days at 2 or 20°C and after dilution to 10-2-10-3
([Brunt & Stace-Smith, 1978*a*](#ref3)).

### Purification

Yields of 4.8 mg virus/kg *Nicotiana clevelandii* leaf tissue are obtainable by the
following procedure
([Brunt & Stace-Smith, 1978*a*](#ref3)).
Extract symptom-bearing leaves
(1 g/3 ml) in 0.25 M borate buffer at pH 7.9 containing 0.01 M 2-mercaptoethanol, partially
clarify the extract by adding chloroform (250 ml/litre), remove the aqueous phase and subject
it to one cycle of differential centrifugation (15 min at 12,000 ***g***; 75 min at
65,000 ***g***). Resuspend the high-speed pellets in 0.05 M borate buffer at pH 7.6
(1 ml/15-30 g leaf tissue). Purify the virus further by chromatography on columns of
controlled-pore glass beads
([Barton, 1977](#ref1)),
followed by chromatography on DEAE cellulose,
eluting with a linear gradient of 0.1-0.5 M sodium chloride in 0.02 M neutral phosphate
buffer; recover virus from the eluates by centrifugation (90 min at 65,000 ***g***)
and resuspend in 0.05 M borate buffer at pH 7.5.

### Properties of Particles

Virus preparations sediment as six components which have sedimentation coefficients
(*s*20,w) of 173, 143, 114, 71, 45 and 15 S
([Brunt & Stace-Smith, 1978*a*](#ref3)).

### Particle Structure

The virus has rod-shaped particles up to 420 nm long but with predominant lengths in
leaf sap of 220-240 nm and 120-140 nm, and a diameter of 21-22.5 nm. The particles are
fragile with fissures at irregular intervals along their lengths; many also have portions
of the primary helix partially disrupted or missing
([Fig. 7](#fig07), [Fig. 8](#fig08)).
Fragmentation often
occurs by discs separating from the particles; these discs can often be seen in end-on view
or still loosely attached to particle fragments
([Brunt & Stace-Smith, 1978*a*](#ref3),
[1978*b*](#ref4)).

### Particle Composition

*Nucleic acid:* The infectivity of crude nucleic acid extracts is inactivated by RNase,
but not DNase, suggesting that the nucleic acid is RNA
([Brunt & Stace-Smith, 1978*a*](#ref3)).

*Protein:* The coat protein subunits have a M. Wt of 24.5 x 103 (estimated
by electrophoresis in polyacrylamide gels containing SDS)
([Brunt & Stace-Smith, 1978*a*](#ref3)).

### Relations with Cells and Tissues

In infected *Nicotiana clevelandii* mesophyll cells, the virus particles are found
scattered throughout the cytoplasm, or loosely aggregated in spherical cytoplasmic masses, or
in regular arrays
([Fig. 6](#fig06)),
or associated with crystalline inclusions
([Fig. 5](#fig05)). The crystalline
inclusions (lattice spacing 9.8 nm) are enclosed in a membrane, are often rectangular in
section and measure up to 2.3 x 1.5 µm; the associated particles are found occasionally
within the crystals, but more frequently are adjacent to the enclosing membrane
([Brunt & Stace-Smith, 1978*b*](#ref4)).

### Notes

Hypochoeris mosaic virus has particles of similar morphology, size and properties to
those of
[broad bean necrosis](/dpv/showdpv/?dpvno=223)
([Inouye & Asatani, 1968](#ref7)),
[beet necrotic yellow vein](/dpv/showdpv/?dpvno=144)
([Tamada & Baba, 1973](#ref12)),
[nicotiana velutina mosaic](/dpv/showdpv/?dpvno=189)
([Randles, Harrison & Roberts, 1976](#ref9)),
[peanut clump](/dpv/showdpv/?dpvno=235)
([Thouvenel, Dollet & Fauquet, 1976](#ref13)),
[potato mop-top](/dpv/showdpv/?dpvno=138)
([Harrison & Jones, 1970](#ref6))
and
[wheat soil-borne mosaic](/dpv/showdpv/?dpvno=077) viruses
([Brakke, Estes & Schuster, 1965](#ref2)),
and to those of
defective strains of
[tobacco mosaic virus](/dpv/showdpv/?dpvno=151)
([Kassanis & Woods, 1969](#ref8)).
It also resembles
some of these viruses in its intracellular location and appearance
([Brunt & Stace-Smith, 1978*b*](#ref4)).
Although hypochoeris mosaic virus is apparently serologically distinct, its
similarities to these labile
[tobamoviruses](/dpv/showdpv/?dpvno=184)
suggests that it too is possibly a member of the
tobamovirus group. It may also, like these labile tobamoviruses, be transmitted by a
root-infecting fungus, but such transmission tests have not yet been attempted.
