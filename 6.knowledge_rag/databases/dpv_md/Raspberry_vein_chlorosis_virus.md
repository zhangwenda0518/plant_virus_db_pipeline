### Introduction

Described by [Cadman (1952)](#ref2). *Synonym*: Raspberry chlorotic net virus (*Rev. appl. Mycol.* **43**: 799) A bacilliform virus with enveloped particles, measuring *c.* 430-500 x 65-80 nm in sections of raspberry. Transmitted by aphids but not by mechanical inoculation of sap. Important in *Rubus* in Europe; reported also in Canada and New Zealand.

### Main Diseases

Natural infections occur in cultivated and wild red raspberry (*Rubus idaeus*).
In most cultivars the virus induces a chlorosis of the minor leaf veins to form a yellow
net pattern which in some cultivars is uniform throughout the young leaves ([Fig. 1](#fig01)) but
in others is patchy ([Fig. 2](#fig02)). Some cultivars also show leaf deformity and epinasty
([Jordovic, 1963](#ref11)); a few show no obvious symptoms of infection.
Stocks of some cultivars such as Baumforth’s B are totally infected.

### Geographical Distribution

Common in Europe
([Cadman, 1952](#ref2);
[Jordovic, 1963](#ref11);
[Richter, 1964](#ref16);
[Bjørnstad, 1958](#ref1);
[Tapio, 1961](#ref23)) and USSR
([Kuznetsova & Pomazkov, 1971](#ref12)). It has also been
introduced into Canada ([Stace-Smith, 1961](#ref19)) and New Zealand
([Cadman & Stace-Smith, 1970](#ref4)) in infected *R. idaeus* cv. Lloyd George.

### Host Range and Symptomatology

The virus has few hosts apart from *R. idaeus.* It has however been transmitted
by grafting to loganberry (*R. loganobaccus*) and by aphids to *Fragaria vesca*
([Stace-Smith, 1961](#ref19)).

*Diagnostic species: R. idaeus* cvs. Lloyd George, Malling Delight, Norfolk Giant and Washington. About 4-8 weeks after inoculation by grafting or by aphids, chlorosis of the minor veins develops in small patches in young expanding leaves ([Fig. 1](#fig01), [Fig. 2](#fig02)). The leaf blade remains normal but may become distorted by uneven growth as the leaf expands. As the leaf ages the symptoms become less distinct and may disappear. Symptoms are less intense in the glasshouse than in the field and depend also on the virus isolate used (see Strains). In cv. Malling Delight, these symptoms are frequently accompanied by epinasty and stunting of young expanding leaves. Interpretation of the reactions of indicator cultivars may be difficult when other viruses are present. : *R. idaeus* (cvs. Cuthbert, Latham and Viking), *R. occidentalis* and *R. henryi* are immune ([Cadman, 1952](#ref2); [Stace-Smith, 1961](#ref19)). : *Fragaria vesca* (Alpine strawberry). Symptoms are similar to those in *R. idaeus* cv. Lloyd George but in addition veinal chlorosis often develops along one of the secondary leaf veins. *Propagation and assay species*: The susceptible *R. idaeus* cultivars listed above are suitable for maintaining cultures and some have also been used for assaying transmission by aphids.

### Strains

[Cadman (1952)](#ref2) distinguished mild,
moderate and severe forms of vein chlorosis in
red raspberry, possibly caused by three different strains of the same virus.

### Transmission by Vectors

Transmitted by *Aphis idaei* but not by the other main aphid vectors of
*Rubus* viruses, *Amphorophora rubi* and *A. agathonica*
([Cadman & Hill, 1947](#ref3);
[Cadman, 1952](#ref2);
[Stace-Smith, 1961](#ref19);
[Jordovic, 1963](#ref11)). Information on
virus-vector relations is scanty but aphids required at least 1 day to acquire the
virus and retained it for at least 1 day
([Cadman, 1952](#ref2);
[Stace-Smith, 1961](#ref19)). Efficiency
of transmission is increased after 7-day acquisition access feeds
([Cadman, 1952](#ref2)), the
greatest recorded frequencies of transmission (46%) being obtained after acquisition
and inoculation access periods of 7 days and 30 days respectively
([Jordovic, 1963](#ref11)).

### Transmission through Seed

Not found in red raspberry ([Jordovic, 1963](#ref11)).

### Transmission by Dodder

None reported.

### Serology

No information available.

### Relationships

Particle morphology places raspberry vein chlorosis virus in the
[rhabdovirus group](/dpv/showdpv/?dpvno=244). It resembles
[lettuce necrotic yellows](/dpv/showdpv/?dpvno=343)
([Francki & Randles, 1970](#ref7)),
[sowthistle yellow vein](/dpv/showdpv/?dpvno=062)
([Peters, 1971](#ref14)),
[broccoli necrotic yellows](/dpv/showdpv/?dpvno=085)
([Campbell & Lin, 1972](#ref5)) and
[strawberry crinkle](/dpv/showdpv/?dpvno=163)
([Sylvester, Frazier & Richardson, 1976](#ref22))
viruses in being aphid-transmitted.

### Stability in Sap

Unlike most other aphid-borne viruses occurring in *Rubus,* it is not
inactivated in infected raspberry plants by exposure to 37°C for several weeks
or months ([Stace-Smith, 1960](#ref18);
[Chambers, 1961](#ref6);
[Jordovic, 1963](#ref11)). However, some virus-free
plants were obtained by rooting excised tips from infected plants that had been kept
at 35°C for 4 weeks ([van der Meer, 1975](#ref24)).

### Particle Structure

In thin sections of infected raspberry tissue bacilliform particles of two kinds
([Fig. 3](#fig03)) are observed:
(a) particles rounded at both ends and measured as *c.* 442-560 x 73-91 nm by
[Stace-Smith & Lo (1973)](#ref20),
and as *c.* 430-500 x 65-80 nm by
[Jones, Roberts & Murant (1974)](#ref10); and (b) shorter particles rounded
at one end. The particles have a densely staining nucleocapsid *c.* 25-30 nm
in diameter surrounded by an electron lucent zone and then by a unit membrane
([Fig. 5](#fig05)) which in some sections appears to be continuous with the endoplasmic reticulum.
The nucleocapsid shows some cross-banding with a periodicity of *c*. 4-5 nm
([Fig. 4](#fig04))
([Stace-Smith & Lo, 1973](#ref20);
[Jones *et al,* 1974](#ref10)).

### Relations with Cells and Tissues

Particles appear to be confined to a small proportion of parenchyma cells of
the vascular bundles. Many cells of this region have wall projections into the
cytoplasm, hypertrophied mitochondria and enlarged perinuclear spaces. Particles
occur singly or in groups in the cytoplasm and perinuclear space
([Fig. 6](#fig06)) but not
in the nucleus and are often found in groups enclosed within a membraneous sac
([Fig. 5](#fig05))
([Stace-Smith & Lo, 1973](#ref20);
[Jones *et al.,* 1974](#ref10)).

### Notes

Virus-like particles and ultrastructural effects resembling those of raspberry
vein chlorosis virus were first found by
[Putz & Meignoz (1972)](#ref15) in raspberry
showing veinbanding mosaic. However, it is now known that of the two viruses
responsible for this disease,
[black raspberry necrosis virus](/dpv/showdpv/?dpvno=333) has spherical particles
*c.* 25 nm in diameter
([Jones & Murant, 1972](#ref8);
[Murant, Jones & Roberts, 1976](#ref13)) and
[rubus yellow net virus](/dpv/showdpv/?dpvno=188) has small bacilliform particles
*c.* 80-150 x 25-31 nm
([Jones & Roberts, 1976](#ref9);
[Stace-Smith & Leung, 1976](#ref21)). Therefore the
plants examined by Putz & Meignoz were probably also infected with raspberry
vein chlorosis virus.

Symptoms induced by rubus yellow net virus in some red raspberry cultivars can
be similar to those induced by raspberry vein chlorosis virus
([Stace-Smith, 1955](#ref17)).
However the viruses are transmitted by different aphid species
([Cadman & Hill, 1947](#ref3)) and have different sizes of particles
([Jones & Roberts, 1976](#ref9)). They can
be distinguished in pure culture by graft indexing to black raspberry (*R. occidentalis),*
which is immune to raspberry vein chlorosis virus but develops
a yellow net symptom on infection with rubus yellow net virus
([Stace-Smith, 1955](#ref17)).
