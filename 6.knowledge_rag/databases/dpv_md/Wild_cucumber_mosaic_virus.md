### Introduction

Described by
[Freitag (1952)](#ref8) and
[Lindberg, Hall & Walker (1956)](#ref12).

An RNA-containing virus with isometric particles about 28 nm in diameter
which sediment as two
components in the ultracentrifuge. It is found only in the USA, has a host range
largely restricted
to the Cucurbitaceae and is transmitted mechanically and by flea beetles. It is
distantly serologically
related to
[turnip yellow mosaic](/dpv/showdpv/?dpvno=230) and
[cacao yellow mosaic](/dpv/showdpv/?dpvno=011) viruses.

### Main Diseases

Causes mosaic in *Marah macrocarpus* (wild cucumber) in California
([Milne, Grogan & Kimble, 1969](#ref16))
and in *Marah oreganus* in Oregon
([Allen & Fernald, 1971](#ref2)).

### Geographical Distribution

USA.

### Host Range and Symptomatology

Largely restricted to the Cucurbitaceae
([Lindberg *et al.,* 1956](#ref12));
however, an Oregon isolate
also symptomlessly infects *Vinca rosea.

Diagnostic species*:   The Californian isolate produces vein-clearing, green blisters and a bright
    yellow mottle in
    *Cucurbita pepo* (pumpkin)
    ([Fig. 1](#fig01),
    [Fig. 2](#fig02))
    and does not infect *Cucumis sativus* (cucumber).
    The Oregon isolate infects *Cucumis sativus* and produces vein-clearing,
    star mottle, wilting
    and necrosis in *Cucurbita pepo* and *Citrullus lanatus*
    (watermelon)
    ([Allen & Fernald, 1971](#ref2)).

    *Propagation species: Cucurbita pepo* (pumpkin). *Assay species*: Some isolates produce local lesions on the cotyledons of *Citrullus vulgaris* (watermelon) ([Lindberg *et al.,* 1956](#ref12)) and *Cucumis melo* (cantaloupe) ([Milne *et al.,* 1969](#ref16)). ### Strains Isolates from California ([Milne *et al.,* 1969](#ref16)) can be distinguished from those of Oregon ([Allen & Fernald, 1971](#ref2)) by host range and symptoms. Two serologically distinguishable strains have been reported from California ([Grogan, Taylor & Kimble, 1964](#ref9)). ### Transmission by Vectors Transmitted by the striped cucumber beetle, *Acalymma trivittata* ([Freitag, 1941](#ref7); [Walters, 1969](#ref22)). ### Transmission through Seed Not tested. ### Transmission by Dodder Not tested. ### Serology The virus is strongly immunogenic. After prolonged immunization, antisera prepared against the virus also react with the depolymerized protein subunits of the virus in gel-diffusion tests ([Bekker, 1967](#ref4)). ### Relationships Distantly serologically related to [turnip yellow mosaic virus](/dpv/showdpv/?dpvno=230) ([MacLeod & Markham, 1963](#ref13); [van Regenmortel, 1966](#ref20)) and [cacao yellow mosaic virus](/dpv/showdpv/?dpvno=011) ([Brunt *et al.,* 1965](#ref5)). The nucleic acid resembles that of turnip yellow mosaic virus in its large cytidylic acid content ([Symons *et al.,* 1963](#ref18)). ### Stability in Sap In *Cucurbita pepo* sap, the virus is inactivated when heated at 70°C for 10 min; sap of infected plants retains its infectivity at room temperature for 28 days. The dilution end-point is 10-4 ([Lindberg *et al.,* 1956](#ref12)). ### Purification Sap from infected pumpkin plants may be clarified by adding ethanol to 20% (v/v) ([Symons *et al.,* 1963](#ref18)), by acidification to pH 4.9 ([Grogan *et al.,* 1964](#ref9)), or by adding bentonite ([Dunn & Hitchborn, 1965](#ref6)). The virus can be precipitated with ammonium sulphate and concentrated by differential centrifugation. Pellets should be resuspended in a buffer because the virus precipitates in the absence of salt. ### Properties of Particles Two major classes of particles occur in purified preparations, ‘empty’ protein shells (top component, T) and infective nucleoprotein (bottom component, B) ([Sinclair, Geil & Kaesberg, 1957](#ref17); [Anderegg *et al.,* 1961](#ref3)). Intermediate components have also been reported ([Yamazaki & Kaesberg, 1961*b*](#ref24); [Markham, 1962](#ref14), [1967](#ref15); [MacLeod & Markham, 1963](#ref13)). The top component is more readily adsorbed by bentonite than is the bottom component; a marked differential in adsorption is observed in 10-3 M MgSO4, 10-2 M phosphate, pH 7.3. Under these conditions the electrophoretic mobility of the two components is also different ([Hitchborn & Dunn, 1965](#ref10)). Sedimentation coefficients (*s*20, w) at infinite dilution (svedbergs): 119 (B), 53 (T) ([Yamazaki & Kaesberg, 1961*a*](#ref23)). Diffusion coefficient (D20, w): 1.27 x 10-7 cm2 sec-1 (T and B). Isoelectric point: pH 6.6 in 0.1 M Na phosphate buffer (T and B). Electrophoretic R Ø value in zone electrophoresis: 0.14 ([van Regenmortel, 1968](#ref21)). Partial specific volume (calculated): 0.740 (T) and 0.670 (B) ([Yamazaki & Kaesberg, 196l*a*](#ref23)). ### Particle Structure The particles are isometric, *c.* 28 nm in diameter ([Fig. 3](#fig03)). The ‘empty’ shells are penetrated by negative stains used in electron microscopy whereas the ‘full’ particles are not. In crude preparations outer shells of 41 nm diameter have been found, associated preferentially with ‘empty’ particles ([Hitchborn & Hills, 1965](#ref11)). ### Particle Composition *RNA:* 35% of particle by weight. Molar percentage of nucleotides: G16.4, A17.0, C41.0, U25.6 ([Symons *et al.,* 1963](#ref18)). *Protein:* 65% of particle by weight. 190 amino acid residues per subunit of Mol. Wt 20,200 ([Symons *et al.,* 1963](#ref18)). ### Relations with Cells and Tissues Distinctive vesicles, similar to those induced by [turnip yellow mosaic virus](/dpv/showdpv/?dpvno=230) ([Ushiyama & Matthews, 1970](#ref19)), develop in the chloroplasts ([Allen, 1972](#ref1)). ### Notes There are numerous similarities between this virus and [turnip yellow mosaic virus](/dpv/showdpv/?dpvno=230), although they have totally different host ranges. Points of similarity include: size and shape, RNA composition, size of protein subunit, common antigenic determinants, beetle vector and induction of chloroplast abnormalities. [Squash mosaic virus](/dpv/showdpv/?dpvno=043) is also found in wild cucumber ([Lindberg *et al.,* 1956](#ref12)) but can be distinguished by serology. ### Acknowledgements Photographs courtesy of J.
