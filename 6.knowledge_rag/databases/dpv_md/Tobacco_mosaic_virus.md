### Introduction

Type member of the genus *Tobamovirus*.
Disease first described in detail and transmitted experimentally by [Mayer](#ref48) in 1886.
Virus concept developed from studies of the disease by [Beijerinck (1898)](#ref6).

Synonyms:

Common strain, wild type, vulgare  
U1 ([Siegel and Wildman, 1954](#ref68))  
OM, Japanese common strain ([Nozu & Okada, 1968](#ref51);
[Watanabe, *et al.*, 1999](#ref79))  
Korean common strain ([Koh *et al*., 1992](#ref42))

  
Brief description:

TMV is a rod-shaped virus, containing a single-stranded RNA molecule of sense polarity.
The virus causes diseases in a broad variety of plant species.
It has no known true vectors, but on rare occasions it is transmitted inefficiently by chewing insects.
It is normally transmitted mechanically.
It is best known for the contributions to virology which have been achieved through studies with TMV
([Harrison & Wilson, 1999](#ref31);
[Scholthof *et al*., 1999](#ref62))

### Main Diseases

Exhibits disease in many plant species ([Holmes, 1939](#ref35)),
although it is not always clear whether it is the type strain or other tobamoviruses which are described.
In tobacco the virus elicits a "...classical mosaic..but may also cause veinbanding and is associated
with a necrotic symptom known as mosaic burn"
([Shew & Lucas, 1991](#ref65)).

### Geographical Distribution

Worldwide

### Host Range and Symptomatology

Host range given in [Holmes (1946)](#ref36).
The virus is known to infect at least 199 species from 30 plant families
([Shew & Lucas, 1991](#ref65)).
Subliminal infections seen in many species
([Cheo & Gerard, 1971](#ref14);
[Sulzinski & Zaitlin, 1982](#ref74)).

*Diagnostic species*: *Nicotiana tabacum* cvs. Turkish, Turkish Samsun, Samsun (Samsoun), White Burley, Burley and Xanthi. Vein clearing appears in young, systemically-invaded leaves, 3-4 days post inoculation, followed by a light green-dark green mosaic, often accompanied by distortion and blistering ([Figure 1](#fig01)). Inoculated leaves exhibit no symptoms other than faint chlorotic lesions when the plant nitrogen supply is limited. Plants may be stunted if they are infected while young. : *N. glutinosa, N.tabacum* cvs. Samsun NN, Xanthi NN and Xanthi-nc, plants with the N genotype, and *Chenopodium amaranticolor, C. quinoa,* and *Phaseolus vulgaris* cv. Pinto, form necrotic lesions which develop at the infection sites ([Figure 2](#fig02)), but without systemic symptoms below about 28o. A systemic necrotic disease can develop above that temperature, in particular when the temperature is subsequently lowered below 28o. *N. clevelandii* and *N.benthamiana* exhibit a local necrosis, followed by systemic necrosis and plant death. *Propagation species*: *N. tabacum*, cvs. Turkish, Turkish Samsun, Samsun or Xanthi (nn). *Assay species*: Local lesion assays are most frequently performed with *N. glutinosa*, *N. tabacum* cvs. Xanthi nc, Xanthi NN, Samsun NN, *Phaseolus vulgaris* cv Pinto, *Chenopodium amaranticolor*or *C. quinoa*.

### Strains

Many isolates once considered to be strains of TMV
([Siegel & Wildman, 1954](#ref68)),
are now separate tobamoviruses in the classification accepted by the
International Committee on the Taxonomy of Viruses, based on recent sequencing information.
There are, however, isolates from Japan
(OM, [Watanabe *et al*., 1999](#ref79)) and from Korea
([Koh *et al*., 1992](#ref42))
with very close sequence homology to the type strain, which allows one to consider them as TMV strains.

### Transmission by Vectors

Virus transmitted principally by mechanical inoculation.
Virus has no true vectors, although there have been reports of incidental
transmission by chewing insects, most probably by mechanical means
([Lojek & Orlob, 1969](#ref45);
[Harris & Bradley, 1973](#ref30)).
Soil-borne virus particles or fragments of infected tissue can serve as sources of infection via roots.
Virus is very persistent on clothing and on glasshouse structures
([Broadbent & Fletcher, 1963](#ref11)).

### Transmission through Seed

Not transmissible via seed or pollen.
However, virus is often present in the seed coat, and there are occasional reports,
principally with tomato, of plants consequently becoming infected by wounding of
the embryo during germination
([Broadbent, 1965](#ref10)).

### Transmission by Dodder

Transmitted, chiefly among tobaccos, by *Cuscuta campestris, C. japonica* 
and *C. subinclusa*, but the particular strains or tobamoviruses have not always been identified.
Virus does not replicate within the dodder.
Subject reviewed by [Hosford, 1967](#ref38).

### Serology

Good immunogen; high titre polyclonal antiserum may be produced from either virions or dissociated coat protein.
Virus detected by a wide range of tests, but most commonly by ELISA tests, involving direct or indirect methods.
Subject reviewed by [Van Regenmortel (1986)](#ref78).
Monoclonal antibodies have been produced
([Briand *et al*., 1982](#ref9)).

### Relationships

There are few true strains of TMV; rather, most are distinct [tobamoviruses](/dpv/showdpv/?dpvno=184).
The relationships are established by sequence comparisons and by serological cross reactions
to the virions themselves or to the coat proteins.
Tobamoviruses with only limited sequence similarity to the type strain do cross-react serologically.
A masked strain inducing very mild symptoms in tobacco has been selected by growing type strain-infected
tomato plants at 34.6oC ([Holmes, 1934](#ref34)).
It is serologically identical to the type strain.
Sequence analysis of the genome shows relatively few changes from the type strain;
the changes which attenuate symptoms are present in the 126-kDa gene
([Holt *et al*., 1990](#ref37)).

### Stability in Sap

Very stable; preparations of "unpreserved plant juice" retained infectivity after 50 years
([Silber & Burk, 1965](#ref70)),
although a temperature-sensitive, nitrous acid-induced mutant is much less stable
([Hariharasubramanian *et al*., 1970](#ref28)).
Very heat stable; some infectivity is retained after 10 minute exposures at over 90o C.
Dilutions of 106 of expressed tobacco sap can be infectious.
Tissue may be preserved by freezing fresh leaves, or by freeze-drying tissue.
Purified virus preparations may be preserved at 4o C for long periods,
using a few drops of chloroform to inhibit microorganisms.

### Purification

Because of its high titre and stability, and its large particle size,
TMV can be purified by many procedures such as ultracentrifugation,
exclusion chromatography, or salt, polyethylene glycol, isoelectric or solvent precipitation.
A method which has been shown to remove host plant contaminants from the virus is given by
[Asselin & Zaitlin (1978)](#ref2).
Virus may be purified from crude preparations by agarose gel electrophoresis
([Asselin & Grenier, 1985](#ref1)).
TMV has the capacity to bind a host nucleoprotein, which can be removed with chelating agents
([Ginoza *et al*., 1954](#ref24)).
Particles may be sorted according to length using columns of agar or agarose beads
([Steere, 1963](#ref73)).
Yields may reach 10 mg.g-1 fresh tissue, but 1-3 mg.g-1 is more common.

### Properties of Particles

Sedimentation coefficient (*s20w*) at infinite dilution is *ca*. 194*S*
([Harrington & Schachman, 1956](#ref29)).
Buoyant density is 1.325 g/cm3 (in CsCl;
[Siegel & Hudson, 1959](#ref67)).
Particle weight is 39.4 x 106 Da
([Caspar, 1963](#ref13)).
Diffusion coefficient (D20,w) is *ca.* 4.4 x 10-8 cm2.sec-1
([Schramm & Bergold, 1947](#ref63)).
Isolectric point is *ca.* pH 3.5
([Fraenkel-Conrat & Narita, 1958](#ref22)).
Partial specific volume is *ca.* 0.73 cm3.g-1
([Lauffer, 1944](#ref44)).
Electrophoretic mobility at ionic strength 0.075 and pH 6.5-7.9 is *ca.* -0.83 x 10-4cm2.sec-1.V-1
([Kramer & Wittmann, 1958](#ref43)).
Extinction coefficient at 260nm, 1 mg.ml-1, 1 cm light path, ranges between 2.7 and 3.5
([Brakke, 1967](#ref8));
a value of 3.0 is commonly used.
*A*260/*A*280 is *ca*. 1.19
([Paul, 1958](#ref57)).

### Particle Structure

Straight, rigid tubules ([Figure 3](#fig03)); length *ca*. 300 nm, max. radius *ca.* 9 nm,
composed of *ca*. 2140 identical protein subunits closely packed in a helix
(pitch *ca.* 2.3 nm, 16 1/3 subunits/turn; [Figure 4](#fig04))
around a cylindrical canal of radius *ca*. 2 nm.
One continuous single strand of RNA of 6395 nucleotides,
follows the same helix (49 nucleotides/turn or 3/subunit) at a radius of *ca*.
4 nm, and is associated with the protein subunits near their inner surfaces
([Caspar, 1963](#ref13))
[[Figure 5](#fig05)].
Particles can be dissociated into constituent nucleic acid and coat protein and reconstituted into
stable infectious virus particles
([Fraenkel-Conrat & Williams, 1955](#ref23)).

### Particle Composition

Nucleic acid:
Single-stranded linear RNA of 6395 nucleotides
([Goelet *et al*., 1982](#ref25)).
Represents *ca*. 5% of particle weight.
Several subgenomic mRNAs (but not the coat protein mRNA) become encapsidated,
resulting in particles of less than full length
([Beachy & Zaitlin, 1977](#ref4)).
Preparation of infectious RNA is described by
[Mandeles & Bruening (1968)](#ref46).

Protein:
About 95% of particle weight is coat protein,
comprised of 2130 identical molecules of 158 amino acids each
([Wittmann-Liebold & Wittmann, 1967](#ref81)).
Amino terminus is acetylated.

Lipid:
None

Other components:
Virions can bind Ca++ and/or Mg++
([Durham & Henry, 1977](#ref20)),
but calcium ion peaks were not seen in X-ray fiber diffraction studies
([Namba *et al*., 1989](#ref50)).
There is about one molecule of ubiquitin per virion,
linked by an isopeptide bond to the coat protein
([Dunigan *et al*., 1988](#ref19)).

Satellite:
An unusual, spherical, RNA-containing satellite virus was isolated from
plants infected with the tobamovirus, tobacco mild green mosaic virus.
TMV will support the satellite virus
([Valverde *et al*., 1991](#ref76)),
but there are no reports of natural occurrence of the satellite virus in association with TMV.

### Genome Properties

Sequence of nucleic acid is archived in GenBank as V01408;
the two sequences have some differences in the 5' untranslated leader
([Goelet *et al*., 1982](#ref25)).
The validity of such differences has been questioned
([Meshi *et al*., 1983](#ref49)).
Sequence of the Korean common strain is archived as
X68110.
There is a m7G5'ppp5'Gp cap on the 5' end
([Zimmern, 1975](#ref84)).
The 3' terminus is a tRNA-like structure with five pseudoknots
([Van Belkum *et al*., 1985](#ref77));
it accepts histidine
([Oberg & Philipson, 1972](#ref52)).
In addition to the genomic RNA, there are 3 subgenomic RNAs
([Figure 6](#fig06)).
The 126-kDa replicase protein and its readthrough of 183-kDa are translated from the
genomic RNA
([Bruening *et al*., 1976](#ref12);
[Hunter *et al*., 1976](#ref39));
readthrough at an amber UAG stop codon is potentiated by two suppressor tRNAs from tobacco
([Beier *et al*., 1984](#ref5)).
The I2-RNA translates into the 30-kDa movement protein
([Bruening *et al*., 1976](#ref12)),
and the LMC RNA is the mRNA for the coat protein
([Hunter *et al.,* 1976](#ref39)).
A third mRNA (I1-RNA) encodes a 54-kDa protein
([Sulzinski *et al*., 1985](#ref75)),
but no corresponding protein has been detected *in vivo*.
The sequences (nt 5420-5546;
[Jonard  *et al.,* 1977](#ref40))
which represent the nucleation site for assembly of the virion are about 1 kb from the 3'
end of the RNA,
within the open reading frame for the 30-kDa protein
([Zimmern, 1977](#ref85)).
Upon entry into the cell, cytoplasmic ribosomes associate with the virion on the 5' end of
the RNA,
and remove coat protein subunits
([Wilson, 1984](#ref80);
[Figure 7](#fig07)).
Infectious transcripts have been generated from cDNA
([Dawson *et al*., 1986](#ref16)).

The purified replicase
([Osman & Buck, 1996](#ref53))
is composed of the 126-kDa and the 183-kDa viral-encoded proteins and a plant protein
related to
the RNA-binding subunit of yeast eIF-3
([Osman and Buck, 1997](#ref54)).
The 126-kDa protein has guanylyltransferase activity
([Dunigan & Zaitlin, 1990](#ref18)).
The 30-kDa movement protein is an early product in the replication process,
but degrades as the replication progresses
([Padgett *et al*., 1996](#ref55)).

The profile of dsRNAs is shown in
[Zelcer *et al*. (1981)](#ref83).

### Relations with Cells and Tissues

Virus replicates in several types of cells including mesophyll, epidermis, root hairs and trichomes.
Particles are mainly in the cytoplasm, but may associate with chloroplasts
([Shalla *et al*., 1975](#ref64))
and with cell walls
([Esau, 1968](#ref21)).
Many virion-like particles within chloroplasts are shorter than full length virions and may be pseudovirions,
which are chloroplast RNAs encapsidated in TMV coat protein
([Siegel, 1971](#ref66)).
Coat protein in chloroplasts associates with thylakoid membranes
([Reinero & Beachy, 1986](#ref59)).

Cells contain crystalline inclusions composed of virus particles
([Figures 8](#fig08) & [9](#fig09)),
and linear aggregates of needles, spindles and fibers.
Amorphous inclusions, also called "X-bodies", are also present
([Figure 10](#fig10)).
Viral-induced replicase
([Osman & Buck, 1996](#ref53); [1997](#ref54))
and dsRNAs are associated with membranes; the 126-kDa replicase protein is found in the "X-bodies"
([Figure 11](#fig11);
[Hills *et al*., 1987](#ref33)).

Movement protein associates with plasmodesmata
([Atkins *et al*., 1991](#ref3)),
and with the microtubules
([Figure 12](#fig12))
and actin filaments of the cytoskeleton
([Heinlein *et al*., 1995](#ref32)).
It modifies the size exclusion limit for molecules to transport through plasmodesmata
([Wolf *et al*., 1989](#ref82)).

The infectious entity which transports the infection from cell to cell via plasmodesmata is RNA
([Siegel *et al*., 1962](#ref69)).
Virus particles
([Saito *et al*., 1990](#ref60);
[Simón-Buela & García-Arenal, 1999](#ref71))
are considered to be required for long distance movement in the host plant.
The infectious entity is transported in the phloem
([Bennett, 1939](#ref7))
but can pass through stem tissue without necessarily replicating there
([Samuel, 1934](#ref61)).
Invasion of minor veins does not require intact virions
([Ding *et al*., 1996](#ref17)).

### Ecology and Control

The virus is known wherever tobacco is grown, and is considered to be one of the most
important tobacco viruses economically
([Gooding, 1991](#ref27)).
Insects are not important in its spread.
Control is by crop rotation and effective sanitation; resistant cultivars of both flue-cured and
burley tobacco are available
([Gooding, 1991](#ref27)).

TMV has been used for the first demonstration of coat protein-mediated resistance
([Powell Abel *et al*., 1986](#ref58)),
replicase-mediated resistance
([Golemboski *et al*., 1990](#ref26))
and movement protein-mediated resistance
([Cooper *et al*., 1995](#ref15)).

### Notes

The type strain used in many laboratories throughout the world apparently has a common origin.
Personal recollections of C.A. Knight, W.C. Price, S.G. Wildman and F.O. Holmes suggest that the
original isolate purified by
[W.M. Stanley (1935)](#ref72)
came from James Johnson of the University of Wisconsin via L.O. Kunkel.
The U1 isolate
([Siegel & Wildman, 1954](#ref68))
and the German isolate 'vulgare'
([Wittmann-Liebold & Wittmann, 1967](#ref81))
also came from Johnson. Japanese
([Watanabe *et al*., 1999](#ref79)) and Korean
([Koh *et al*., 1992](#ref42))
common strains apparently have independent origins.

An anthology reprinting 25 seminal articles (with commentaries),
in which TMV studies have been shown to contribute basic knowledge to virology in general,
and to plant virology specifically, is edited by
[Scholthof *et al*. (1999)](#ref62).
A TMV historical volume commemorating the 100 year contribution of TMV studies,
as presented in a symposium in Edinburgh in 1998, is edited by
[Harrison & Wilson (1999)](#ref31).
