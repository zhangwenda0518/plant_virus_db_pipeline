### Introduction

Described by [Jensen (1949*a*)](#ref13), [Conover (1962](#ref5), [1964*a*)](#ref6), [De Bokx (1965)](#ref10) and [Zettler, Edwardson & Purcifull (1968)](#ref22). *Synonyms* The following incompletely studied viruses may be synonyms:: Papaya (papaw) distortion ringspot virus (*Rev. appl. Mycol.* **41**: 530): Papaya (papaw) mosaic virus (*Rev. appl. Mycol.* **28**: 131) A virus with flexuous, filamentous particles about 800 nm long. It is stylet-borne by aphids, is sap-transmissible, and has a narrow host range. Causes a disease of major importance in papaya.

### Main Diseases

In papaya, it causes mottling and distortion of leaves, rings and spots on fruit,
and streaks on
stems and petioles. It stunts the plants and diminishes fruit production
([Conover, 1964*a*](#ref6)).

### Geographical Distribution

In most tropical and subtropical areas of the world where papayas are grown.

### Host Range and Symptomatology

Host range is narrow; 11 species in three dicotyledonous families
(Caricaceae, Chenopodiaceae and
Cucurbitaceae) have been experimentally infected, but papaya is the only reported
natural host.
Sap-transmissible.

*Diagnostic species: Carica papaya*(papaya). Symptoms in papaya are variable. Seedlings show vein-clearing and downward cupping of the young systemically infected leaves about 2 weeks after inoculation. After several weeks, leaves may become mottled ([Fig. 3](#fig03)) and deformed, the lobes being markedly reduced in size ([Fig. 1](#fig01)). Among other species of *Carica* tested, some were susceptible and some were not ([Conover, 1964*a*](#ref6)). : *Cucurbita pepo* (summer squash and pumpkin). Yellow chlorotic areas appear along minor veins in systemically infected squash leaves 10-14 days after inoculation ([Fig. 5](#fig05)), followed by mottle and some leaf distortion. Symptoms in pumpkin are similar, but generally milder. *Propagation species*: Papaya, squash and pumpkin are useful for maintaining cultures. Pumpkin has been used as a source of virus for purification ([Milne & Grogan, 1969](#ref16)). *Assay species: Chenopodium amaranticolor*develops local lesions ([Cook & Milbrath, 1971](#ref9)). Pumpkin is useful for vector studies.

### Strains

A minor variant designated ‘faint mottle ringspot’ induces milder symptoms in
*Carica papaya*than does the strain common in Florida
([Conover, 1964*a*](#ref6),
[1964*b*](#ref7)).

### Transmission by Vectors

Transmitted by several species of aphids in a non-persistent manner, notably *Myzus persicae*
([Jensen, 1949*b*](#ref14);
[Conover, 1964*a*](#ref6);
[Zettler *et al.,* 1968](#ref22)).
Other vectors are
*Aphis gossypii, A. medicaginis, A. rumicis, Macrosiphum solanifolii* and *Micromyzus
formosanus*
([Jensen, 1949*b*](#ref14)).

### Transmission through Seed

None detected in papaya
([De Bokx, 1965](#ref10)).

### Transmission by Dodder

No information.

### Serology

An antiserum was obtained by injecting rabbits with clarified sap from infected papaya leaves
([Story & Halliwell, 1969*b*](#ref21)).
Liquid precipitin tests with purified virus and
immunodiffusion tests with detergent-treated virus are useful for studying
serological relationships
([Milne & Grogan, 1969](#ref16)).

### Relationships

The virus is serologically related to
[watermelon mosaic virus](/dpv/showdpv/?dpvno=293)
([Milne & Grogan, 1969](#ref16))
but the
degree of relationship is not clear; its general properties place it in the
[potato virus Y](/dpv/showdpv/?dpvno=242)
group of
viruses
([Brandes & Bercks, 1965](#ref2)).

### Stability in Sap

In papaya sap, the virus loses infectivity after 10 min at 54-56°C and after 8 hr at room
temperature. The dilution end-point is about l0-3
([Conover, 1964*a*](#ref6)).

### Purification

Partially purified virus for serological tests was obtained from infected pumpkin leaves
using a
method developed for
[watermelon mosaic virus](/dpv/showdpv/?dpvno=293)
([Milne & Grogan, 1969](#ref16)).
Tissue was homogenized in
0.3 M potassium phosphate buffer, pH 7.6 (2 ml/g tissue). The extract was clarified with
*n*-butanol and the virus obtained by two cycles of differential centrifugation.

### Properties of Particles

No information.

### Particle Structure

Flexuous filaments
([Fig. 6](#fig06))
800 nm long and 12 nm in diameter
([Herold & Weibel, 1962](#ref11)).

### Particle Composition

No information.

### Relations with Cells and Tissues

Cytoplasmic inclusions
([Fig. 2](#fig02))
have been observed in stained epidermal strips of squash
([Christie, 1971](#ref4)).
Pinwheel inclusions
([Fig. 4](#fig04))
are seen in the cytoplasm of infected tissues
([Zettler *et al.,* 1968](#ref22);
[Story & Halliwell, 1969*b*](#ref21)).
In negatively stained extracts
from infected leaves the striated nature of these inclusions is revealed
([Fig. 7](#fig07)).
Virus particles
have been observed in the cytoplasm of infected cells
([Herold & Weibel, 1962](#ref11)).

### Notes

Numerous mosaic virus diseases of papaya have been reported from various parts of the world
([Adsuar, 1947](#ref1);
[Capoor & Varma, 1958](#ref3);
[Ishii & Holtzmann, 1963](#ref12);
[Kulkarni, 1970](#ref15);
[Namba & Kawanishi, 1966](#ref17);
[Pozdena, 1967](#ref18);
[Singh, 1969](#ref19)).
They resemble papaya ringspot in some respects, but
the precise relationship of the causal viruses has not been determined. They should
not be confused,
however, with the
[papaya mosaic virus](/dpv/showdpv/?dpvno=056)
described by
[Conover (1962](#ref5),
[1964*b*](#ref7)),
[De Bokx (1965)](#ref10)
and
[Zettler *et al.* (1968)](#ref22).
Papaya mosaic virus is a flexuous rod 530 nm long, is not
aphid-transmitted, and is a member of the
[potato virus X](/dpv/showdpv/?dpvno=354)
group
([Brandes & Bercks, 1965](#ref2)).
[Watermelon mosaic virus](/dpv/showdpv/?dpvno=293),
to which papaya ringspot virus is serologically related, does not infect
papaya
([Milne & Grogan, 1969](#ref16)).

A mycoplasma-like organism has been associated with the leafhopper-borne
bunchy top disease of
papaya
([Story & Halliwell, 1969*a*](#ref20)).
The virus diseases of papaya have been reviewed
recently by
[Cook (1972)](#ref8).
