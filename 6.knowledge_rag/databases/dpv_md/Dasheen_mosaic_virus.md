### Introduction

Described by [Zettler *et al*. (1970)](#ref26) and [Abo El-Nil, Zettler & Hiebert (1977)](#ref2). A virus with flexuous, filamentous particles *c*. 750 nm long. It is transmitted by aphids in the non-persistent manner, is sap-transmissible, and infects members of the family Araceae.

### Main Diseases

Causes mosaic and/or distortion diseases of *Aglaonema* spp., *Caladium hortulanum*(caladium), *Colocasia* spp. (dasheen, taro), *Dieffenbachia* spp. (dieffenbachia,
dumbcane), *Xanthosoma* spp. (cocoyam, elephant’s ear, tannia, yautia) and *Zantedeschia*spp. (calla lily).

### Geographical Distribution

Appears to be world-wide, especially in tropical and subtropical
regions. Reported from the Caribbean ([Alconero & Zettler, 1971](#ref4); [Debrot & Ordosgoitti,
1974](#ref7); [Hartman,
1974*a*](#ref10)), Egypt ([Abo El-Nil & Zettler, 1976](#ref1)), Florida, USA ([Hartman & Zettler,
1972](#ref12); [Zettler *et al.*, 1970](#ref26)),
Europe ([Hakkaart & Waterreus, 1976](#ref9); [Van Hoof, 1972](#ref24); [Zettler *et al.*, 1970](#ref26)), India
([Hartman, 1974*a*](#ref10)),
Japan ([Arai, Doi & Yora, 1970](#ref5)) and Oceania ([Alconero & Zettler, 1971](#ref4); [Buddenhagen,
Milbrath & Hsieh,
1970](#ref6); [Gollifer *et al.*, 1977](#ref8); [Kenten & Woods, 1973](#ref17)).

### Host Range and Symptomatology

Reported to infect species of the following 13 genera in the family Araceae:   
*Aglaonema,
Alocasia, Amorphophallus, Anthurium, Arisaema, Caladium, Colocasia, Cryptocoryne, Dieffenbachia,
Philodendron, Spathiphyllum, Xanthosoma, Zantedeschia* ([Hartman, 1974*a*](#ref10); [Hartman &
Zettler, 1972](#ref12); [Tooyama, 1975*a*](#ref22); [Zettler *et al.*, 1970](#ref26)).

*Diagnostic species: Philodendron selloum*. Young seedlings show systemic vein chlorosis *c*. 2-3 weeks after inoculation ([Fig. 1](#fig01)). This symptom is usually followed by severe mosaic and deformation of subsequently formed leaves ([Fig. 2](#fig02)). : *Caladium hortulanum, Colocasia esculenta* or *Xanthosoma caracu*. Foliar mosaic symptoms, often localized. Mosaic patterns may appear as conspicuous localized ‘feathering’ along the veins ([Fig. 3](#fig03)). Many leaves of infected plants may be symptomless ([Tooyama, 1975*b*](#ref23); [Zettler *et al.*, 1970](#ref26)). : *Dieffenbachia picta*. Leaves show mosaic and distortion symptoms. In some instances, the unfurling leaf may be distorted. Mature leaves may be torn due to uneven expansion rates of laminar tissues. Leaves may be symptomless during certain parts of the growing season ([Hakkaart & Waterreus, 1976](#ref9); [Hartman, 1974*a*](#ref10)). : *Zantedeschia*. Leaves show mosaic and distortion symptoms. *Propagation species: Philodendron selloum*. Seedling plants are useful as a source for virus purification and for maintaining virus cultures. *Assay species: Philodendron verrucosum*is reported to be a local lesion host ([Tooyama, 1975*a*](#ref22)). *Philodendron selloum* is a useful test plant for aphid transmission tests.

### Strains

None distinguished, although minor differences in symptoms induced by single isolates from
Florida and Fiji in *Philodendron selloum* have been noted ([Abo El-Nil *et al.*, 1977](#ref2)).

### Transmission by Vectors

Transmitted in a non-persistent manner by aphids including *Myzus persicae* and *Aphis
craccivora*, but not apparently by *Pentalonia nigronervosa* ([Morales & Zettler, 1977](#ref20)).

### Transmission through Seed

None detected. Seedlings of *Caladium hortulanum, Dieffenbachia picta* and *Xanthosoma
caracu* derived from infected parental stock were healthy ([Hartman *et al.*, 1972](#ref13); [Volin
& Zettler, 1976](#ref25)).

### Serology

The virus is a good immunogen. It reacts well in immunodiffusion tests conducted in agar gels
containing sodium dodecyl sulphate ([Fig. 4](#fig04)) or in agar gels after pyrrolidine treatment of the
antigen ([Abo El-Nil *et al.*, 1977](#ref2)). The proteinaceous cylindrical inclusions are also
immunogenic, are detectable in agar gels containing sodium dodecyl sulphate, and are immunochemically
distinct from viral coat protein (F. J. Morales, F. W. Zettler & E. Hiebert, unpublished data).

### Relationships

This virus has been classified in the [potyvirus group](/dpv/showdpv/?dpvno=245) on the basis of particle morphology,
aphid-transmissibility, ability to induce cylindrical (‘pinwheel’) inclusions in its hosts,
and serological relationships to other potyviruses ([Abo El-Nil *et al.*, 1977](#ref2); [Zettler *et
al.*, 1970](#ref26)). Immunodiffusion tests conducted in agar gels containing sodium dodecyl sulphate
indicate that this virus is related to [blackeye cowpea mosaic](/dpv/showdpv/?dpvno=305) and [tobacco etch viruses](/dpv/showdpv/?dpvno=258) ([Abo El-Nil
*et al.*, 1977](#ref2)).

### Stability in Sap

In sap from infected *Philodendron selloum*, the virus was inactivated by heating to
60-65°C for 10 min by dilution beyond 10-2, or by storage for more than 75 h at
26°C ([Alconero, 1972](#ref3)).

### Purification

The virus has been purified from systemically infected leaves of *Philodendron selloum* 
harvested 2-3 weeks after inoculation. Homogenise tissue (100 g) in a chilled mixture
of 200 ml 0.1 M sodium citrate (pH 7.2) containing 0.6 g sodium sulphite and 0.01 M disodium ethylene
diamine tetraacetate, 45 ml chloroform, and 45 ml carbon tetrachloride. Centrifuge the homogenate
at 13,200 ***g*** for 10 min. Precipitate the virus from the supernatant fluid by adding
polyethylene glycol M. Wt 6000 to 8% (w/v) and purify further using differential centrifugation and
equilibrium centrifugation in CsCl ([Abo El-Nil *et al.*, 1977](#ref2)).

Virus-induced cylindrical inclusions of this virus can be obtained from the tissue extract after
treatment with chloroform and carbon tetrachloride (F. J. Morales, F. W. Zettler & E. Hiebert,
unpublished data). Clarify the homogenate by centrifugation at 4000 ***g*** for 5 min then
centrifuge the supernatant fluid at 13,200 ***g*** for 15 min. Resuspend the resulting
pellet in 0.02 M phosphate (pH 8.2) containing 0.1% mercaptoethanol and add Triton X-100 to 5% (v/v)
and stir for 1 h. To remove the detergent, use two cycles of centrifugation at 20,000
***g*** for 20 min. Resuspend the pellet and further purify the inclusions by sucrose density
gradient centrifugation ([Hiebert & McDonald, 1973](#ref15)).

### Properties of Particles

Absorbance at 260 nm (1 mg/ml, 1 cm light path): 2.38.

*A*260/*A*280: 1.09-1.19.

### Particle Structure

Particles are flexuous filaments ([Fig. 5](#fig05)), about 750 nm long ([Zettler *et al.*, 1970](#ref26)).

### Particle Composition

*Nucleic acid:* No information.

*Protein:* Electrophoresis of virus coat protein in 11% polyacrylamide gels containing sodium
dodecyl sulphate revealed up to six components ranging in estimated M. Wt from 25,000 to 45,000.
The major components, the two largest resolved, showed anomalous electrophoretic behaviour,
giving much higher estimates of M. Wt in 3% and 6% than in 11% gels ([Abo El-Nil *et al.,* 1977](#ref2)).

### Relations with Cells and Tissues

The virus induces cytoplasmic cylindrical inclusions which can be detected by light microscopy
([Fig. 6](#fig06)) and electron microscopy ([Fig. 7](#fig07), [Fig. 8](#fig08)) ([Abo El-Nil & Zettler, 1976](#ref1); [Zettler *et al.*,
1970](#ref26)) The cylindrical inclusion protein subunit has a M. Wt of *c.* 69,000 (F. J.
Morales, F. W. Zettler & E. Hiebert, unpublished data).

### Notes

Filamentous viruses infecting *Anthurium andraeanum* in Venezuela ([Herold, 1967](#ref14)) and
*Zantedeschia aethiopica* in the Soviet Union ([Kolbasina & Protsenko, 1973](#ref18)) have been
reported, but their relationship to dasheen mosaic virus has not been determined. [Cucumber mosaic
virus](/dpv/showdpv/?dpvno=213) ([Lovisolo & Conti, 1969](#ref19)), [tomato spotted wilt virus](/dpv/showdpv/?dpvno=039) ([Tompkins & Severin, 1950](#ref21)) and
two bacilliform viruses infecting taro ([James, Kenten & Woods, 1973](#ref16)) can readily be distinguished
from dasheen mosaic virus by differences in particle morphology, host range and/or mode of transmission.

The widespread incidence of dasheen mosaic virus reported in plantings of *Aglaonema, Caladium,
Colocasia, Dieffenbachia, Xanthosoma* and *Zantedeschia* ([Abo El-Nil & Zettler, 1976](#ref1);
[Alconero & Zettler, 1971](#ref4); [Debrot & Ordosgoitti, 1974](#ref7); [Hartman & Zettler, 1972](#ref12); [Zettler
*et al.*, 1970](#ref26)) probably results from its transmissibility by aphids and the use of vegetative
means for propagating these plants. Tissue culture techniques have been used to obtain virus-free
plants of *Caladium, Colocasia*, and *Xanthosoma* ([Hartman, 1974*b*](#ref11)). Attempts to
eliminate dasheen mosaic virus from *Xanthosoma* corms by heat treatment were unsuccessful
([Alconero, 1972](#ref3)).
