### Introduction

Described by
[Van der Meer, Huttinga & Maat (1976)](#ref8).

A virus with isometric, RNA-containing particles about 27 nm in diameter, which
sediment as two components. Detected only in lilac from which it is readily transmissible
by inoculation of sap to several herbaceous hosts. Seed-borne in some herbaceous hosts.
Vector unknown. Reported only from the Netherlands.

### Main Diseases

Causes rings and line patterns on lilac (*Syringa vulgaris*)
([Fig. 1](#fig01));
these, however, cannot be distinguished from symptoms caused by other
viruses of lilac
([Van der Meer, 1976](#ref6);
[1978](#ref7)).

### Geographical Distribution

Reported only from the Netherlands.

### Host Range and Symptomatology

Host range seems to be fairly wide although not
many species have been tested. Besides lilac, species of Chenopodiaceae, Amaranthaceae,
Leguminosae, Solanaceae and Apocynaceae are susceptible (
[Van der Meer *et al.,* 1976](#ref8))
Readily transmissible by inoculation of sap.

*Diagnostic species: Celosia argentea.*Faint chlorotic lesions followed by a bright systemic chlorosis. : *Chenopodium quinoa.* Systemic epinasty after 6 to 20 days followed by severe stunting ([Fig. 2](#fig02)); often there is tip necrosis. : *Nicotiana tabacum* (tobacco), type White Burley, and *N. glutinosa.* Transient wilting of tip leaves ([Fig. 3](#fig03)). *Propagation species: Chenopodium quinoa. Assay species*: The virus may be assayed by recording systemic infections in whole plants of *Chenopodium quinoa*. No local lesion host is known.

### Strains

Isolates differing in the severity of symptoms induced in herbaceous
hosts have been noted. No serological differences have been detected.

### Transmission by Vectors

No vectors are known. Not transmitted by *Myzus
persicae* 
([Van der Meer *et al.,* 1976](#ref8)).

### Transmission through Seed

No seed transmission was detected in lilac
([Van der Meer, 1976](#ref6)).
However, seeds from infected plants of *Chenopodium quinoa,
Chenopodium amaranticolor* and *Celosia argentea* gave rise to 13, 91 and
24% infected plants, respectively
([Van der Meer *et al.,* 1976](#ref8)).

### Serology

Strongly immunogenic in rabbits. An antiserum with a titre of
1/1024 has been prepared. In the Ouchterlony double diffusion test the antiserum
reacted with virus preparations purified from *Chenopodium quinoa* and also
with crude extracts of *C. quinoa* and lilac
([Van der Meer *et al.,* 1976](#ref8)).

### Relationships

The properties, structure and composition of its particles
indicate that lilac ring mottle virus belongs to the
[ilarvirus group](/dpv/showdpv/?dpvno=275),
but no
serological relationship was detected to four members of this group, namely
[apple mosaic](/dpv/showdpv/?dpvno=083),
[elm mottle](/dpv/showdpv/?dpvno=139),
[prunus necrotic ringspot](/dpv/showdpv/?dpvno=005) and
[tobacco streak](/dpv/showdpv/?dpvno=307) viruses.

### Stability in Sap

In *Chenopodium quinoa* sap, the infectivity dilution
end-point is about 10-4, the thermal inactivation point (10 min) is about
63°C, and infectivity is retained for 3 to 5 h at 20°C.

### Purification

Harvest leaves from infected *Chenopodium quinoa* with
severe symptoms and store overnight at 4°C. Disrupt 100 g portions of leaf in
a blender with a mixture of 35 ml carbon tetrachloride, 35 ml chloroform, 15 ml
diethyl ether and 270 ml 0.018 M phosphate-citric acid buffer, pH 7, containing
0.1% thioglycollic acid. Centrifuge the homogenate at low speed. Concentrate the
virus from the upper phase by precipitation with 8% (w/v) polyethylene glycol
M. Wt 6000 and two cycles of differential centrifugation. Purify further by rate
zonal centrifugation in sucrose gradients, or, after fixation with 2% glutaraldehyde,
isopycnic centrifugation in CsCl or Cs2SO4. Throughout the
purification keep the virus in 0.018 M phosphate-citric acid buffer, pH 7
([Van der Meer *et al.,* 1976](#ref8)).

### Properties of Particles

Purified preparations contain two centrifugal
components
([Fig. 4](#fig04)),
top (T) and bottom (B). B is infective, T is not, but it can
slightly enhance the infectivity of B
([Van der Meer *et al.,* 1976](#ref8)).

Sedimentation coefficients (in 0.018 M phosphate-citric acid, pH 7, at 20°C
and at infinite dilution) (svedbergs): 83 (T) and 98 (B).

Buoyant densities of T and B are the same: 1.345 g/cm3 in CsCl and
1.265 g/cm3 in Cs2SO4.

Electrophoresis in 2.6% polyacrylamide gels separates the virus into a faster
moving band, corresponding with T, and a slower moving one corresponding with B
([Van der Meer *et al.,* 1976](#ref8);
[Huttinga & Mosch, 1976](#ref2)).  

*A*260/*A*280: 1.43 to 1.46 for preparations containing both
components, or either alone.

### Particle Structure

Particles are very sensitive to buffers of high ionic
strength and must be fixed with glutaraldehyde before staining with phosphotungstate
for electron microscopy. The virus then appears as irregularly shaped isometric
particles with some heterogeneity in size
([Fig. 5)](#fig05). Average particle diameter is
27 nm
([Van der Meer *et al.,* 1976](#ref8)).

### Particle Composition

*Nucleic acid:* RNA, single-stranded. Based on *A*260/*A*280
ratios the amount of RNA in the virus particles is estimated to be 13-14%.
Unfractionated preparations contain RNA species with M. Wt, estimated by
polyacrylamide gel electrophoresis, of 1.18 x 106 (RNA-1), 1.13 x
106 (RNA-2), 0.90 x 106 (RNA-3) and 0.37 x 106
(RNA-4) (H. Huttinga & W. H. M. Mosch, unpublished data). T contains only
RNA-3 and RNA-4; B contains predominantly RNA-1 and RNA-2, but also a small amount
of RNA-4 and some RNA-3. All four RNA species are necessary for infection, but RNA-4
can be replaced by the coat protein
([Huttinga & Mosch, 1976](#ref2)).

*Protein:* T and B particles contain a single species of polypeptide, of M.
Wt 27,500 ([Huttinga & Mosch, 1976](#ref2)).

### Relations with Cells and Tissues

No information.

### Notes

By back transmission from herbaceous hosts to lilac seedlings, it has
been shown that at least four viruses detected in lilac, namely
[arabis mosaic](/dpv/showdpv/?dpvno=016),
lilac ring mottle,
[tobacco rattle](/dpv/showdpv/?dpvno=346)
([Van der Meer, 1976](#ref6)) and
[elm mottle](/dpv/showdpv/?dpvno=139)
([Schmelzer, 1969](#ref4)),
cause rings and/or line patterns in lilac. It is not known whether five other viruses
detected in lilac,
[cherry leaf roll](/dpv/showdpv/?dpvno=306),
[tomato bushy stunt](/dpv/showdpv/?dpvno=069)
([Novak, 1977](#ref3)),
[tomato black ring](/dpv/showdpv/?dpvno=038)
([Schmelzer, 1970](#ref5);
[Van der Meer, 1978](#ref7)),
[lilac chlorotic leaf spot](/dpv/showdpv/?dpvno=202)
([Brunt, 1978](#ref1))
and
[strawberry latent ringspot](/dpv/showdpv/?dpvno=126)
([Van der Meer, 1976](#ref6)), can cause similar symptoms.
However, from many lilac plants with rings and line patterns on the leaves, none of
these nine readily sap-transmissible viruses could be isolated
([Schmelzer, 1970](#ref5);
[Van der Meer, 1976](#ref6);
[Novak, 1977](#ref3)), suggesting that at least one other virus causes rings
and line patterns in lilac. Lilac ring mottle virus is therefore identified only by
transmission to herbaceous indicators and by serological tests.
